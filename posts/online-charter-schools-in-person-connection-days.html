Online Charter Schools Add In-Person Days—Here's Why

# Online Charter Schools Are Solving the Socialization Problem—With Monthly In-Person Days

The fastest-growing corner of public education isn't a new curriculum framework or a flashy edtech platform. It's a model that has quietly matured since the pandemic accelerated its adoption: the fully online public charter school that deliberately, systematically brings students together in the physical world.

This isn't a contradiction. It's becoming a blueprint.

Online Charter Enrollment Has Passed a Tipping Point

In 42 states and Washington, D.C., online public schools have been established by state authorities, school districts, or charter schools, meaning students can attend them full-time and tuition-free across all grades K–12. The numbers behind that footprint are striking: approximately 564,000 K–12 students attended a full-time online school during the 2022–23 school year.

That's not a pandemic anomaly. Past federal data shows a near-doubling from 478 virtual public schools in 2013 to 691 by the 2019–2020 academic year. And state-level enrollment figures suggest the trend has continued well past the emergency conditions that first pushed families online. As Robin Lake, director of the Center on Reinventing Public Education, put it: "A number of parents and students were kind of forced into the option, liked it, and wanted to continue. And many teachers found they really loved the scheduling flexibility and location flexibility."

Families choose virtual charter schools for myriad reasons. At their core, these schools offer students and their families the ability to tailor learning experiences to specialized needs—geographic isolation, medical needs, students who were bullied, athletes with intensive training schedules, and learners who simply thrive when they can move at their own pace.

The One Question Every Virtual School Has to Answer

If enrollment momentum is real, so is the challenge that comes with it. Ask any parent considering an online charter school what concerns them most, and the answer is almost always the same: What about socialization?

What is the socialization concern with online charter schools, and how are leading schools addressing it?

The concern is legitimate and straightforward: without daily hallway interactions, lunch tables, and after-school activities, will students miss the peer relationships and collaborative skills that traditional schools naturally provide? The better virtual charter schools have stopped treating this as an unsolvable tension and started engineering around it. Many virtual schools now offer in-person and virtual events that build school pride and encourage student interaction, alongside extracurricular clubs and activities that often take place in person, even when classroom instruction does not.

The key word is structured. Occasional field trips or one-off events don't build community. Regular, calendared, teacher-attended in-person gatherings do.

How Monthly Connection Events Are Changing the Model

The most thoughtful online charters are building recurring in-person gatherings directly into the academic rhythm of the school year—treating community-building as part of the program, not an optional add-on.

Frontier Charter, a tuition-free, nonprofit public K–12 online charter school serving students across Oregon, is a clear illustration of this approach. Rather than leaving socialization to chance, the school holds monthly "Frontier Days"—in-person meetups at museums, parks, and other educational venues—where students build friendships, explore new experiences, and connect face-to-face with their teachers and classmates. Every enrolled student receives a Chromebook along with access to Google Apps for Education and the Canvas learning platform, and instruction is delivered by highly-qualified, Oregon-licensed teachers. The school was founded by educators, parents, and community members committed to pairing modern technology with meaningful, authentic learning—and the monthly in-person events are a deliberate extension of that founding philosophy, not an afterthought.

That design choice reflects something the research increasingly supports: online instruction and in-person community are not opposites. They are complements, and schools that treat them that way produce a more complete student experience.

Why Charter Status Makes Full Implementation Possible

There is a structural reason why online charter schools are often better positioned to execute this integrated model than district-run virtual programs. RAND research found that charter teachers and students reported a greater extent of using and experiencing competency-based practices—such as being able to work on different topics than others and at their own pace—and that key components of flexible learning environments, including flexible use of space and staff and use of technology, were more common in charter schools than in district-run schools.

That flexibility extends well beyond the classroom. Charter status allows schools nearly complete freedom to hire and manage their staff, design their instructional programs, and shape their professional learning—all without consulting district administrators. For an online school trying to coordinate monthly statewide in-person events while simultaneously managing an asynchronous learning platform, ensuring state teacher licensure compliance, and maintaining charter accountability targets, that operational autonomy isn't a minor perk. It's what makes the whole model viable.

AI-assisted tools now keep track of how each student is doing and adjust lessons to fit their needs—especially helpful for kids who learn differently—while freeing teachers to spend less time on paperwork and more time actually teaching. Pair that kind of adaptive instructional infrastructure with a consistent in-person community rhythm, and you have a school model that is genuinely distinct from both traditional brick-and-mortar schools and the first-generation "click through a course" virtual schools of the 2010s.

Policy Is Starting to Catch Up

Legislatures and authorizers are beginning to recognize that online charter schools need both appropriate accountability frameworks and the freedom to run events-based programming. Texas passed legislation requiring public schools to advertise their online offerings to families and to offer extracurricular activities to online students—a signal that policymakers increasingly see structured connection as a core service, not a bonus feature. Meanwhile, Mississippi recently approved its first hybrid charter school, set to open for the 2025–26 school year, offering a combination of online and in-person instruction in a county whose district had received a D rating for student performance.

In Oregon, a 3% per-district enrollment cap on virtual charter schools limits how many students can access these programs, and advocates have argued the cap restricts families from enrolling in schools that may better meet their children's needs. That policy tension is unlikely to ease as demand for flexible learning options continues to grow.

What Charter Leaders and Authorizers Should Look For

Not every online charter is executing the connection model thoughtfully. Before enrolling a student—or approving a charter application—here are the markers that distinguish genuine community-building from a checkbox:

  • Regularity: Are in-person events monthly, or is there a single annual gathering?
  • Geographic accessibility: Can students from across the school's full service area realistically attend?
  • Academic integration: Do the events connect to curriculum content, or are they purely social?
  • Teacher presence: Are licensed educators actively participating and building real relationships with students at these events?
  • Transparency: Is the events calendar published and available for prospective families to review before they enroll?

Schools that score well across all five of these criteria are solving the socialization challenge in a durable, replicable way. Schools that don't are leaving a gap that attentive families will eventually notice—and leave over.

The Broader Lesson for Charter Innovation

The online-plus-connection model may be the most quietly replicable form of charter school innovation right now. It doesn't require a major philanthropic investment or a flagship urban campus. It requires organizational clarity about what school is actually for—academic mastery, yes, but also the relationships and experiences that help young people become capable, curious, and connected people.

Virtual charter schools that have figured out how to deliver both are worth studying closely. The ones that haven't yet are sitting on a meaningful competitive and pedagogical opportunity—and they don't have to look far for models that are already making it work.
