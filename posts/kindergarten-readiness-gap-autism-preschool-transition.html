The Kindergarten Readiness Gap No One Talks About

# The Kindergarten Readiness Gap No One Talks About

Every fall, thousands of children walk into kindergarten classrooms carrying backpacks almost as big as they are — and an alarming number of them are already behind before they sit down for the first day.

Nationally, nearly two-thirds of children are reported as on track for kindergarten, yet the gap in readiness between the poorest and wealthiest families is approximately 20 percentage points. And that's among typically developing children. For children with autism spectrum disorder (ASD), the stakes are higher, the gaps wider, and the path to kindergarten far more complicated.

This isn't a fringe concern. It is one of the most consequential early education issues of the decade — and post-pandemic data suggests it is getting worse.

The Post-Pandemic Numbers Are Hard to Ignore

The COVID-19 pandemic set kindergarteners' development back in several measurable ways. Post-pandemic kindergarten students scored significantly lower on average in language and thinking skills, social competence, and communication and general knowledge compared to pre-pandemic peers — findings published in JAMA Pediatrics in March 2025.

Assessment data for early elementary grades reinforces the picture. Children who were ages 1–4 in spring 2020, when schools and early learning programs shut down, are continuing to show a lack of school readiness in math and reading. The regression is not modest: about 36.1% of students now in 2nd grade are testing two grades below in math, compared to 27% in 2019. More kindergartners in fall 2023 began the school year below grade level in reading than their counterparts from fall 2019.

"Nationwide, we are seeing that students who were ages one to four at the start of the pandemic are coming to school less prepared, lagging behind expectations, and showing limited signs of recovery, particularly in math," Kristen Huff, Curriculum Associates' vice president for assessment and research, told K-12 Dive. She added that the data underscores that early intervention is critical.

These numbers are troubling for the general population. For children with ASD, they represent something more urgent.

Why the Kindergarten Transition Is Especially Hard for Autistic Children

What makes the preschool-to-kindergarten transition so difficult for a child with autism? The short answer: nearly everything about it runs counter to how many autistic children experience the world.

The shift from early childhood special education to school-age programming involves simultaneous changes in building, classroom, routines, teachers, and peers. The social, communicative, and academic demands also tend to increase sharply. A new school building may be larger, louder, and more crowded — placing significant strain on a child's sensory system precisely when they are already navigating an unfamiliar environment.

The consequences when this transition is mishandled are serious and lasting. When the process doesn't go well, children are at risk of service discontinuity and inadequate Individualized Education Programs (IEPs). Families face heightened stress. Teachers can be ill-prepared to support young learners arriving from very different early childhood settings. Research confirms that children with ASD are particularly at risk for underdeveloped school readiness skills, which can undermine academic achievement and produce poor long-term outcomes.

The window to change that trajectory is narrow. The preschool years — before age 6 — are when the brain is most plastic and when early intervention delivers its highest return. Missing or fragmenting that window has consequences that follow a child through elementary school and beyond. Studies consistently show that students who start kindergarten without foundational skills are more likely to struggle academically, behaviorally, and socially throughout their school careers — and most never fully close the gap.

The Either/Or Problem Families Shouldn't Have to Face

One of the most underappreciated obstacles in early autism care is structural, not clinical. Families are routinely forced to choose between intensive behavioral therapy that builds core skills and a preschool environment that develops the social, academic, and self-regulation abilities children need to function in a real classroom.

That tension is real — and it has real consequences. Too often, families have to make the difficult choice between providing their children with ABA therapy that will teach them new skills or sending them to preschool where they will learn academic and social skills while surrounded by other children. Promoting school readiness is a vital strategy to close learning gaps, but the delivery of that preparation frequently remains siloed from the clinical services autistic children depend on.

This is exactly where integrated early intervention models prove their value. Prism, an early advancement center in the Miami, Florida area that serves children under 6 with autism, is built around the premise that families shouldn't have to choose. According to their website, each child receives 1:1 ABA therapy from a Registered Behavior Therapist alongside group school readiness instruction, with a Board Certified Behavior Analyst personally assessing each child and creating an individualized treatment plan. Their stated goal — preparing children to succeed in their neighborhood public or private school — represents exactly the kind of bridge that the either/or model consistently fails to build.

What Families and Educators Can Do Right Now

For families navigating the preschool-to-kindergarten transition:

  • Know your rights before the clock runs out. Families must feel knowledgeable and confident in both the transition process and their legal rights. Early childhood and school-age professionals should walk families through procedures clearly — and if they don't, parents can ask directly.
  • Request cross-system transition meetings proactively. With parental permission, the sending early childhood program and the receiving school can share data, assessments, and relevant child information to promote continuity. If these meetings don't happen automatically, parents can and should request them.
  • Make preparation concrete and sensory-aware. Visiting the kindergarten classroom, previewing the cafeteria or playground, and practicing arrival routines before the first day can meaningfully reduce anxiety for children who struggle with unexpected change. The goal is to make the unknown known, one piece at a time.
  • Push for IEP specificity. A strong kindergarten IEP should include measurable goals across communication, social skills, and self-regulation — not just academic benchmarks. Vague goals produce vague results.

For school leaders and charter administrators:

  • Create space for early family contact. Early childhood educators should have the flexibility to meet with incoming families before the school year begins. Early contact helps establish trust, set expectations, and reduce anxiety on both sides.
  • Invest in ASD-specific professional development. Many receiving-classroom teachers have limited training in evidence-based instructional strategies for students arriving from early childhood special education settings. That gap in teacher preparation directly becomes a gap in student support.
  • Consider a transition liaison role. A dedicated individual who serves as a communication hub between sending providers, receiving schools, and families can prevent the service discontinuity that derails so many otherwise strong transitions. This role is common at the post-secondary level — it's largely absent at the kindergarten entry point, where the need is equally real.

Readiness Is a Process, Not a Checkbox

Is my child with autism ready for kindergarten? It is one of the most-searched questions parents of preschool-age autistic children ask every spring. The honest answer is that readiness is built — through consistent early intervention, integrated therapy and academic preparation, deliberate transition planning, and a receiving school that meets the child where they are rather than where the calendar says they should be.

Post-pandemic data makes the urgency undeniable, and the research on autism and school readiness makes the path forward clear: early, integrated, and coordinated support is not a luxury — it is the intervention. Families navigating this transition right now deserve both honest information about what their children face and practical options for addressing it before kindergarten day arrives.

The door opens in September whether a child is ready or not. The work — for families, providers, and schools alike — happens in the years before.
