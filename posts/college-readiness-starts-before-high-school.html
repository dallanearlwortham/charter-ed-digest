Why College Readiness Must Start Before High School

# Why College Readiness Must Start Before High School

Dual enrollment is having a genuine moment. In 2023–24, roughly 2.8 million high school students—approaching one in three—took at least one college-level course for credit, a 12.7% jump over the prior year. All 50 states now have some form of dual enrollment policy on the books, a milestone completed in 2025 when New York passed landmark legislation that finished the map. Charter schools have been among the most enthusiastic adopters, using dual enrollment partnerships as a differentiator in a competitive school-choice landscape.

But a quieter question is gaining traction among researchers and school leaders: What happens before the dual enrollment class? The evidence increasingly points to a clear answer—students who arrive at dual enrollment academically and emotionally prepared are the ones who actually benefit. And building that preparation is a K–8 job, not a twelfth-grade scramble.

The Dual Enrollment Boom Is Real—and Uneven

The growth numbers are striking. Community colleges now enroll dual-credit high schoolers who make up roughly 20% of their total student population. Approximately 90% of U.S. high schools offer at least one dual enrollment option. States are investing heavily: tuition waivers, articulation agreements, equity grants, and workforce-aligned course sequences are spreading fast.

Yet the research tells a more complicated story beneath those headline figures. Participation and success rates lag significantly for Black, Latino, English-learner, and students with disabilities. Programs that close those gaps share a common trait: they don't wait until junior year to get students ready. They start much earlier—with advising, academic habits, and a college-going identity that students have internalized long before they ever set foot in a community college classroom.

Researchers who have studied equity gaps in dual enrollment are direct about the fix: "starting program outreach earlier, even in middle school" is one of the key structural solutions for making sure these programs benefit all students, not just those who already arrive well-prepared.

What "College Ready" Actually Means in Grades K–8

What does college readiness look like for elementary and middle school students?

College readiness at the K–8 level isn't about early AP credits or accelerated testing. It's about building the foundational architecture that makes rigorous coursework possible later:

  • Academic vocabulary and reading stamina — the ability to engage with dense, complex texts without shutting down
  • Growth mindset and academic self-efficacy — the belief, reinforced by consistent feedback, that effort produces results
  • Study and organizational habits — time management and self-monitoring skills that college-level work demands
  • Character and persistence — the capacity to work through difficulty and keep going
  • College-going identity — an internalized sense that higher education is a realistic, expected destination, not a distant possibility

None of these develop in a semester. They develop over years of deliberate practice inside a school culture that treats college preparation as the core mission—not an elective add-on for the top quarter of students.

How Charter Schools Are Structuring the Pipeline

Charter schools have a structural advantage here: mission focus. A traditional district school serves everyone and often diffuses its energy across competing priorities. A college-preparatory charter school can align every grade, every advisory period, and every family communication around a single north star.

Solare Collegiate Charter School, a tuition-free K–8 public charter school in southwest Albuquerque, New Mexico, is a clear example of this philosophy in action. Its mission—equipping all students with the academic and personal skills to excel in the high school and college of their choice, through individualized support, character development, and academic rigor—reflects precisely what the research says matters most: it names both the academic and personal dimensions of readiness, commits to doing it for all students rather than a self-selected cohort, and starts that work in kindergarten. By the time a Solare student reaches high school and encounters a dual enrollment opportunity, the college-going identity and academic habits needed to succeed in that course have had years to take root.

This is what researchers mean when they describe the preparation that actually makes dual enrollment work—and it's a model worth studying regardless of what state your school is in.

The Risk of Skipping the Foundation

Charter school leaders exploring dual enrollment partnerships face a tempting shortcut: sign the articulation agreement, market the college-credit opportunity to prospective families, and let enrollment applications climb. The problem is that dual enrollment without adequate preparation can backfire badly.

Students who enroll in college courses without sufficient academic scaffolding are more likely to earn failing or incomplete grades—grades that land on a permanent college transcript before they've ever graduated high school. For first-generation students and those from under-resourced communities, a rough early college experience doesn't just cost tuition; it can erode confidence and redirect students away from higher education entirely.

Analysts of dual enrollment's risks are blunt about what they call "random acts of dual credit"—scattered classes that don't build toward a credential, don't transfer to a four-year institution, and don't connect to any meaningful career pathway. The antidote isn't better dual enrollment marketing. It's a coherent, multi-year college-readiness curriculum running from the earliest grades through the moment a student walks into their first college classroom.

What Charter Leaders and Families Should Look For

Whether you lead a charter school or are choosing one for your child, here's what distinguishes a genuine college-readiness pipeline from a program that only looks like one:

  • Mission language that names all students — not just high achievers or those who "self-select" into college-prep tracks
  • Character development integrated with academics, not siloed as a separate "soft skills" program
  • Multi-year advisory or mentorship structures that track individual students' progress toward readiness benchmarks across grades
  • Family engagement that introduces college vocabulary and planning early — before middle school ends, not during senior year
  • Vertical alignment in curriculum so that skills compound year over year rather than resetting with each new teacher or grade

For families evaluating charter options, the right question isn't only "Does this school offer AP or dual enrollment?" It's the earlier question: "What does this school do in grades K through 8 to ensure my child is genuinely prepared to succeed when those opportunities arrive?"

The Bottom Line

Dual enrollment is one of the most promising and well-researched tools in K–12 education right now—proven to accelerate credential completion, reduce college costs, and improve persistence when implemented well. But it works well only for students who arrive prepared. That preparation is a years-long process, and the charter schools doing it best understand that college readiness isn't a high school program. It's a school-wide culture, built one grade at a time, starting long before anyone hands a student a college course catalog.

The dual enrollment milestone numbers are worth celebrating. The harder, quieter work—building the foundation underneath them—is where the real opportunity lies for charter leaders, policymakers, and the families navigating these choices.
