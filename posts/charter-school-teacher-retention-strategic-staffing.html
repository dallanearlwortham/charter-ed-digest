Why Charter Schools Struggle to Keep Great Teachers

Charter Schools Face a Teacher Retention Crisis—and the Stakes Are High

The achievement gains charter schools are known for depend entirely on one thing: keeping skilled teachers in the building year after year. That's getting harder. Turnover rates are higher in charter schools (17.8%) than in traditional public schools (14.9%), according to a recent Learning Policy Institute analysis—a gap that quietly undermines everything from classroom culture to long-term student outcomes.

The problem is compounding. A shrinking talent pipeline is a primary driver, with the education field experiencing a 19% decrease in professionals earning bachelor's degrees in education over recent years. Fewer candidates means longer vacancies, which means existing staff absorb more responsibilities—and burn out faster. Nearly 59% of teachers report experiencing burnout, and 55% of educators end up leaving the profession earlier than planned.

For charter school leaders working to close opportunity gaps, this is an urgent operational challenge, not just an HR inconvenience.

Why Teachers Leave—and Why Most "Fixes" Fall Short

Money matters, but it's rarely the whole story. Only one-third of teachers feel their pay is adequate, and low pay is the number-one reason educators consider quitting. Yet raising salaries in isolation doesn't solve the underlying problem. Research consistently shows that teacher salary increases must be paired with career support, manageable workloads, and workplace respect to actually move the needle on retention.

The deeper issue is structural. Most schools still ask teachers to do the same job with the same title, the same pay range, and the same ceiling—regardless of how long they stay or how effective they become. Nearly 1 in 7 educators leave their classrooms every year amid limited opportunities for growth in salary and responsibility. When there's no ladder to climb without leaving the classroom entirely, talented teachers either stagnate or walk. And notably, 84% of educators want to remain in an instructional role—they're not trying to escape teaching; they're trying to find a version of it that's sustainable.

What the Research Says Actually Works

Strategic Staffing: Redesigning the Teaching Role Itself

The most promising developments in teacher retention aren't coming from compensation studies alone—they're coming from schools that have fundamentally rethought what the teaching job looks like.

Opportunity Culture, a model developed by Public Impact and now active in more than 1,150 schools, redesigns teacher roles so that highly effective educators take on leadership responsibilities and earn higher pay—without being pulled out of the classroom. Multi-Classroom Leaders (MCLs) coach teams of teachers while maintaining their own student roster, earning an average of 20% additional base pay. Students taught in these models benefit from nearly a half-year of additional learning gains. After implementing Opportunity Culture, the teacher vacancy rate decreased 17 percentage points in Ector County, TX schools—a result that no salary bump alone could have produced.

The ASU Next Education Workforce (NEW) model takes a team-based approach: instead of solo practitioners, small teams of teachers share a roster of students and collectively manage instruction, grouping and regrouping students based on need rather than schedule. After implementing ASU NEW models, Mesa Public Schools in Arizona found that teamed teachers were more likely to be rated highly effective and experienced a 50% decrease in teacher vacancies within two years. Broader research on team-teaching structures found teacher turnover dropped from 22% to just 6.6% among teachers with genuine team authority.

The throughline across both models: when teachers feel professionally supported, structurally valued, and part of something larger than their own isolated classroom, they stay.

Professional Development as a Retention Investment

Districts that prioritize coaching and mentoring report higher retention at an 84% rate. Early-career support matters most—structured induction and ongoing professional development during a teacher's first three to five years are among the highest-leverage investments any school can make. A two-day August orientation is not enough; the schools retaining teachers long-term are building systems that support them continuously through the early years when attrition risk is highest.

This is where charter school networks have a real structural advantage over standalone schools. A network operating multiple campuses can build shared professional learning communities, develop internal pipelines for leadership roles, and deploy instructional coaches across buildings in ways a traditional public school simply cannot replicate.

Networks like Noble - Baker College Prep—one of 17 tuition-free public charter schools in Chicago serving grades 6–12—illustrate this approach: the Noble Schools network explicitly recruits through a structured Teaching Residency program and dedicated Talent Pathways, investing in teacher development from the point of entry rather than scrambling to retain people on the way out the door.

A Practical Retention Checklist for Charter Leaders

What can charter schools do right now to reduce teacher turnover?

The most effective retention strategies combine competitive compensation with career growth structures that don't require teachers to abandon the classroom to advance. Schools that design differentiated instructional roles with differentiated pay, invest in mentoring, reduce administrative burden, and build collaborative team models consistently outperform peers on retention. Addressing workload and professional autonomy matters as much as salary—43% of teachers say they would stay if their workload improved.

Here's where to start:

  • Audit your attrition data. Track not just who left, but when—and look for patterns by grade level, content area, or years of service. You can't fix what you haven't mapped.
  • Build visible career pathways. Mentor teacher, instructional coach, department lead, multi-classroom leader—give teachers a ladder that doesn't require becoming an administrator to climb.
  • Differentiate pay for differentiated roles. If teachers carry heavier instructional leadership responsibilities, compensation should reflect that explicitly, not symbolically.
  • Protect collaborative planning time. Teachers who work in structured teams with shared planning report significantly higher job satisfaction and stay longer. Guard this time as fiercely as instructional time.
  • Invest in induction, not just onboarding. Structured, ongoing support through the first two to three years is where retention battles are won or lost.
  • Survey staff—and follow through. The data already tells you that most teachers want to stay in the classroom. Your job is to make that actually sustainable for them.

---

The Connection to Student Achievement Is Direct

Every teacher who leaves mid-year or doesn't return in the fall costs a charter school in dollars, in instructional continuity, and in the trust of the students and families who chose that school deliberately. Researchers from Harvard and Stanford flagged in their 2025 Education Scorecard that students are in a "learning recession," with average math and reading scores still meaningfully below 2019 levels. Teacher instability is a direct contributor to that slide—and stabilizing the teacher workforce is one of the most concrete levers charter leaders can pull.

The good news is that proven models exist, the data is clear, and the charter sector's structural flexibility makes implementation more feasible here than almost anywhere else in K–12 education. What's required is the organizational will to treat teachers as the career professionals they are—and to build systems that reflect that commitment before the resignation letter arrives.
