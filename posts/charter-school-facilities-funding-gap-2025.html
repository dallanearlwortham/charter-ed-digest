The Charter Facilities Gap Is Finally Getting Fixed

# The Charter Facilities Gap Is Finally Getting Fixed

For decades, charter school leaders have played an impossible budgeting game: stretch per-pupil dollars meant for teachers and curriculum to also cover rent, building debt, and maintenance — costs that traditional public schools rarely have to think twice about because local bonds and property taxes handle them automatically. That arrangement quietly shortchanges hundreds of thousands of students every year. In 2025, a wave of state legislatures decided they had seen enough of it, and the results are the most significant facilities-funding breakthrough the charter sector has seen in years.

The Size of the Problem

The numbers behind the charter facilities gap are stark. Charter schools receive, on average, 30 percent less funding per student than traditional public schools, driven largely by a lack of access to local funding streams. A significant slice of that gap traces directly to facilities: traditional districts often receive dedicated local revenue for school buildings and maintenance, but charter schools rarely share in those funds — meaning charter leaders must constantly redirect dollars intended for instruction toward rent, construction, or facility debt.

That redirection has real consequences inside classrooms. Every dollar a charter principal spends on a landlord is a dollar not spent on a reading specialist, a science lab, or an extended learning program. A University of Arkansas study found that charter schools across 18 cities in 16 states receive about 30 percent — roughly $7,147 in 2020 dollars — less funding per pupil than traditional public schools. Over two decades, that disparity has remained stubbornly stable. The gap shows up in practical, grinding ways that charter leaders know well: signing commercial leases at full market rates, delaying building repairs to protect payroll, or turning away waitlisted students because the physical space simply cannot expand. Facility access is consistently cited as the number-one barrier to high-quality charter growth.

What Changed in 2025

The 2025 state legislative sessions produced what advocates are calling a breakthrough year for charter facilities equity. Across the country, states generated $251 million in new funding for charter school facilities. Eight states — Florida, Idaho, Indiana, Missouri, Ohio, Tennessee, Texas, and Utah — passed laws creating or significantly expanding that funding stream.

The single biggest move came from Texas. Governor Abbott signed HB 2 into law on June 4, 2025, removing a longstanding $60 million annual cap on facilities funding for public charter schools and dramatically increasing the state's investment. The result: a $100 million annual increase in charter school facilities funding that will benefit the more than 436,000 students currently enrolled in Texas public charter schools.

Tennessee took a different structural approach, committing $20 million in per-pupil facility aid alongside $5 million to launch the Tennessee Equitable Facilities Fund — a revolving loan program designed to give charter schools access to the kind of long-term capital financing that district schools take for granted. Colorado raised its Moral Obligation Program cap to $1 billion and secured full Mill Levy Equalization funding, while Florida removed barriers to enrollment increases and ensured charter schools have access to local infrastructure funding.

At the federal level, momentum is building in parallel. The Equitable Access to School Facilities Act, introduced in Congress in 2025, aims to make it easier for charter schools to secure and afford high-quality learning spaces by supporting direct grants, revolving loan funds, reserve accounts, and alternative ownership models. A companion federal grant competition — the State Charter School Facilities Incentive Grant program — awarded three new state grants in September 2025, with future competitions anticipated.

What "Facilities Equity" Actually Looks Like in Practice

What is the charter school facilities funding gap, and why does it matter?

The facilities funding gap refers to the structural disadvantage charter schools face because most state funding formulas cover only operational costs, not capital expenses. Traditional public schools can issue bonds backed by local property taxes to build or renovate buildings; charter schools generally cannot access those same mechanisms. The result is that a charter school serving the same students as a nearby district school may spend $1,000–$2,000 more per pupil per year simply keeping the lights on and the roof intact — money that never reaches a classroom. Closing this gap means more instructional dollars stay where they belong: with students and teachers.

Schools that do manage to secure a permanent, purpose-built home consistently see the payoff in program quality and long-term stability. Excel Academy Charter School, a network serving East Boston and Chelsea, Massachusetts — communities that are predominantly low-income and Latino and that have historically faced significant educational barriers — navigated exactly this challenge over its first decade of operation. The school pieced together MassDevelopment bond financing and credit guarantees to build and ultimately own its facilities, freeing up operational funds that could be reinvested directly in students rather than handed to a landlord. That kind of persistent, creative financing is currently the norm for high-performing charter schools, not the exception — and it is precisely the burden that the 2025 legislation is designed to lift.

What Charter Leaders Should Do Right Now

The legislative wins of 2025 don't automatically flow into your school's budget. Capturing them requires deliberate action. Here is where to focus:

  • Audit your state's current policy. Confirm whether your state is among the eight that passed new laws in 2025, and read the specific mechanics carefully — per-pupil aid formulas, revolving loan eligibility thresholds, and enrollment caps all vary significantly from state to state.
  • Watch federal grant deadlines. The U.S. Department of Education's State Charter School Facilities Incentive Grant program funds states, which then pass financing benefits downstream to individual schools. Knowing whether your state has applied — or plans to — affects your own planning horizon.
  • Engage your authorizer early. New facilities funding streams often come with eligibility requirements tied to charter renewal terms and performance ratings. High-performing schools with longer charter terms are typically better positioned to qualify for long-term facility financing, and some states now allow high-performing charters to hold renewal terms of up to 15 years.
  • Connect with CDFIs and nonprofit lenders. Even where state policy has not yet changed, Community Development Financial Institutions remain a critical bridge for charter facility financing — particularly for schools in low-income communities where conventional commercial lenders are reluctant to engage.
  • Model the real cost of your space. Before pursuing any financing, calculate what your school currently redirects from instructional budgets to cover facility costs. That number is your negotiating baseline and your case to authorizers, donors, and policymakers.

The Larger Picture

The 2025 legislative wave matters beyond the dollar amounts. It represents a growing bipartisan recognition that funding formulas built for a district-only world do not adequately serve the millions of families who have chosen a different path. Charter school advocates secured major wins this year, advancing funding equity, facilities access, and growth opportunities while expanding high-quality public options in states from Colorado to Tennessee to Texas.

The facilities gap did not appear overnight, and it will not close in a single session. Structural inequities that have compounded over two decades will take sustained legislative attention to fully resolve. But for the first time in years, the policy momentum is clearly moving in the right direction — and charter leaders who understand the landscape will be best positioned to capture every dollar that flows from it.
