The Charter Facilities Gap: Why 2025 Changed Things

# The Charter Facilities Funding Gap — And Why 2025 May Be the Year It Finally Starts to Close

Every charter school leader knows the math is harder than it looks. You're running a public school, serving public school students, and operating under public accountability standards — but for years, the check that arrives hasn't matched what neighboring traditional district schools receive. The difference isn't small. And for most charter schools, the gap lives squarely in one place: facilities.

That's starting to change. The 2025 state legislative sessions produced a wave of charter school facilities funding wins that advocates have been building toward for years. Understanding what happened — and what it means for your school's budget and your students' experience — is essential reading for any charter leader or engaged family right now.

Why Facilities Funding Is the Core of the Charter Funding Debate

When people talk about charter schools being underfunded, the facilities question is almost always at the heart of it. Traditional districts often receive dedicated local revenue for school buildings and maintenance — local bond elections, millage levies, capital funds that never appear on the district's instructional budget. Charter schools rarely share in those local funding streams.

The result is a structural imbalance that compounds year after year: charter schools must redirect dollars intended for instruction toward rent, construction, or facility debt. Every dollar diverted to a lease payment or a building repair is a dollar that isn't going toward a teacher's salary, a reading intervention, or an after-school program. It's a quiet tax on charter school quality — one families typically don't see until it shows up in program cuts or staffing shortages.

Research has put hard numbers to what most charter leaders feel in their bones. Studies of major urban markets have found an average per-pupil funding gap of roughly 29 percent between charter schools and traditional district schools — a disadvantage that, in some cities, exceeds $5,000 per student annually. That gap has not historically been shrinking on its own.

What "Facilities Funding" Actually Means in Practice

Can't a charter school just find a building and rent it? Yes — and that is precisely the problem. When a charter school rents space, that rent comes out of the same per-pupil operating allotment that pays teachers and purchases curriculum. Traditional public schools, by contrast, access separate capital funds that don't touch their instructional budget.

The practical result is that some of the most innovative schools in the country are operating out of converted strip malls, shared church spaces, or aging commercial buildings — not by design, but by financial necessity. Instructional programming gets squeezed to cover the mortgage or the landlord. Charter leaders spend meaningful time on real estate problems that district principals never have to think about.

That patchwork creates enormous inequality from one ZIP code to the next, and it means two students sitting in structurally identical classrooms — one in a charter, one in a district school — may be generating very different levels of public investment.

2025: A Turning Point at the State Level

In 2025, state legislatures across the country moved on this problem in a way that hadn't been seen before. The National Alliance for Public Charter Schools documented major policy wins in at least thirteen states during the year's legislative sessions — all advancing funding equity, facilities access, or growth opportunities for charter schools. The breadth of that list is striking: Colorado, Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Illinois, Indiana, Missouri, North Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Washington, and Wyoming all recorded meaningful progress.

A few examples illustrate the range of approaches:

  • Tennessee secured $20 million in new per-pupil facility aid and launched a $5 million revolving loan fund specifically for charter facilities financing — giving schools access to low-cost capital they couldn't previously reach.
  • Wyoming eliminated a statewide cap on new charter approvals and simultaneously secured equitable access to state facilities funding for existing schools.
  • Illinois passed its first-ever dedicated state facilities funding for charter schools, after years in which charters operated with no state building support whatsoever.
  • Texas delivered what may be the single largest state-level charter facilities win of the legislative cycle — and the story behind it is worth understanding in detail.

Texas as a Case Study: From a $60 Million Cap to $255 Million

For years, Texas capped the total state facilities funding available to charter schools at $60 million annually — a ceiling that hadn't kept pace with enrollment growth or the cost of real estate in the state's booming cities. Texas charter schools were documented as receiving $1,621 less per student than traditional independent school districts, a gap driven substantially by that facilities shortfall.

House Bill 2, authored by Rep. Brad Buckley and signed by Governor Abbott on June 4, 2025, removed that cap entirely and reset the state's annual investment from $60 million to $255 million — a more than fourfold increase that will support public school choice options and help charter schools meet growing family demand for seats.

The Texas Public Charter Schools Association — the Austin-based statewide advocacy organization whose mission is to advance student achievement by strengthening and advocating for diverse, high-quality charter schools in Texas — called HB 2 "a milestone years in the making" in its 2024–25 annual report. The organization's years of policy research, coalition-building, and unified legislative advocacy on behalf of the 436,000 students enrolled in Texas public charter schools were central to the outcome. The $100 million annual increase in facilities funding the bill delivers represents a shift from what had been an arbitrary ceiling to a formula that actually reflects what charter schools need to serve students well.

What This Means for Charter Leaders and Families Right Now

The 2025 legislative wins are significant — but they don't run on autopilot. Here's what they mean in practical terms:

For charter school leaders:

  • Know your state's updated funding formulas. Per-pupil facilities allotments, equity supplements, and new revolving loan programs all require active engagement and budget planning. The money won't find your school; you have to go find the money.
  • Model the long-range impact now. If your state increased facilities support, this is the year to project what a multi-year reduction in facility-related debt service could look like — and what freed-up dollars could do for staffing or programming.
  • Stay engaged with your state association. The 2025 wins happened because state associations built research records, organized advocates, and sustained pressure through long legislative cycles. Your school's voice in that process is not optional.

For families evaluating charter schools:

  • Ask directly whether a school owns or leases its space, and whether it receives any dedicated state facilities funding. A school with stable, affordable space is one whose budget is more fully aimed at what happens inside the classroom.
  • Look at a school's financial audit. Rent and debt service as a percentage of total expenses can tell you a great deal about how much instructional flexibility a school actually has.

The Equity Argument Isn't Settled — But the Momentum Has Shifted

The 2025 legislative wins are real and consequential, but they don't end a debate that has been running for three decades. Advocates argue that funding equity is a basic matter of fairness: the same child, enrolled in a charter or a district school, should generate comparable public resources for their education. Others argue that charter operators accepted these trade-offs when they signed their charters, or that adding new obligations complicates already-stressed district finances.

What's not debatable is the downstream effect. When a charter school diverts instructional dollars to pay a landlord, students in that school receive less — less staffing capacity, less programming, less of the investment that drives outcomes. The 2025 state actions that expanded and equalized facilities funding were, at their core, about redirecting money back toward teachers, classrooms, and kids.

Whether that momentum continues into 2026 legislative sessions will depend on whether charter advocates — school leaders, families, and statewide associations alike — stay organized and keep making the case. Based on what happened in 2025, the case is clearly getting through.

Facilities funding is one of the most consequential — and most underreported — fault lines in K–12 education finance. If you lead a charter school or are choosing one, it's worth understanding not just what a school teaches, but what it takes to keep the lights on.
