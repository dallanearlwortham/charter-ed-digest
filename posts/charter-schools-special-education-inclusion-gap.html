Charter Schools & Special Ed: Closing the Gap

The Quiet Gap No Charter Leader Should Ignore

Nearly every charter school in the country markets itself on a promise: a better education for every child. But a growing body of research points to a persistent blind spot — students with disabilities are enrolling in charter schools at meaningfully lower rates than in traditional public schools, and even when they do enroll, the quality of their experience varies widely.

That gap isn't inevitable. And for charter school leaders serious about equity, closing it may be one of the most consequential decisions of the next decade.

What the Numbers Actually Show

The share of charter school students who qualify for special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act has grown steadily — from 10.4% in 2012 to 11.5% in 2021. That upward trend sounds encouraging until you compare it to traditional public schools, where the rate stood at 14.1% in 2021, nearly three full percentage points higher.

That enrollment gap, if the trend continues, could produce a significant equity divide in the next decade for historically marginalized students who also have a disability, according to a multi-year study by the Center for Learner Equity, a nonprofit that advocates for inclusive and equitable education for students with disabilities in public schools.

The stakes are concrete. Students with disabilities represent roughly one in eight students in the charter sector today, and that share is growing. Charter schools were built to disrupt educational inequity. That mission doesn't hold up if it stops at the schoolhouse door for kids with IEPs.

Why Charter Schools Are Falling Short — and Why They Don't Have To

Charter school stakeholders — including state policymakers, authorizers, the schools themselves, and related support organizations — can do more to ensure students with disabilities receive high-quality special education services, according to the Center for Learner Equity research. Charter school authorizers, for instance, could better leverage their oversight role to help close opportunity gaps for students with disabilities.

To date, the research found, charter schools have failed to "consistently address the needs of students with disabilities or, perhaps more importantly, close the gap in educational outcomes between students with disabilities and their peers." Left unaddressed, this can have long-term negative consequences on students' academic, mental, and behavioral development.

But here is the counterintuitive truth buried in the same research: because of their autonomy, charter schools are actually well-positioned to create more sustainable teaching and learning systems for students with disabilities than many district schools. The same flexibility that lets a charter set its own schedule, hire outside traditional certification pathways, and build a distinctive school culture can be directed — deliberately — toward better inclusion. The problem isn't that charters lack the tools. It's that too few have chosen to use them this way.

What High-Performing Charter Schools Do Differently

Including students with disabilities in general education classrooms, building a rigorous hiring process, and prioritizing teacher retention were the most impactful factors for student success, according to 59 interviews researchers conducted with school leaders, teachers, parents, technical assistance providers, and paraprofessionals at 16 charter schools and six charter management organizations. Here is what the most effective schools are doing in practice:

Hire with inclusion as a stated criterion. Teachers at high-performing schools described how their hiring committees asked direct questions about candidates' approaches to educating students with disabilities and used role-play scenarios to assess them in real time. These exercises reveal whether a general education teacher views students with disabilities as a shared responsibility — or someone else's problem.

Retain the teachers you invest in. Robust retention practices identified in the research include meaningful professional development, personal support structures, and thorough new-teacher onboarding that builds a common culture focused on underserved students. Crucially, the study found that investing in retention helps address the chronic shortage of qualified special education candidates by building that expertise from within.

Integrate special education into general education. The most successful schools dismantled the wall between special and general education. Some did this by allowing all students — not just those with IEPs — access to both remedial and accelerated content. Others incorporated co-teaching into all core instructional courses, so students with disabilities were learning alongside peers rather than being pulled out as a default.

Use scheduling autonomy as an inclusion tool. One participating charter shifted to a block schedule and added two hours each day for staff collaboration, instructional planning, and IEP development. That structural change allowed special and general education teachers to work alongside therapists, behavioral specialists, and other service providers in ways a rigid traditional schedule would never permit.

What does a high-performing charter school do for students with disabilities? The research is clear: it integrates, hires intentionally, retains its best educators, and builds structured time for cross-disciplinary collaboration. These are not boutique practices reserved for large, well-funded networks. They are systemic choices any school — regardless of size or budget — can make once leadership treats inclusion as a priority rather than a compliance checkbox.

The Role of State-Level Support

Individual schools cannot solve this alone. The research on Washington state charters is instructive: charters there enroll a greater share of students with disabilities and are more likely to educate them in general education classrooms compared to the national average. School leaders and teachers attributed those outcomes not just to school-level will, but to supportive organizations that provide technical assistance, promote equity-minded hiring, and help build inclusive school cultures across the sector.

That is the connective tissue that statewide associations provide. Organizations like the North Carolina Assocation for Public Charter Schools — whose mission centers on support, education, and advocacy for charter schools across the state — are positioned to disseminate exactly this kind of research, connect leaders facing common challenges, and advocate for policies that make inclusion more achievable at the school level. When a state's charter ecosystem is well-organized, individual principals don't have to reinvent the wheel on hard problems like special education compliance and inclusive instructional design.

Questions Charter Leaders and Families Should Be Asking Now

If you lead a charter school, these questions belong on your leadership agenda:

  • What percentage of your enrolled students have IEPs, and how does that compare to your local district's rate?
  • Are your general education teachers hired and evaluated with disability inclusion explicitly in mind?
  • Do your special and general education staff have protected, structured time to collaborate?
  • Is special education programming a meaningful factor in your school's self-evaluation and charter renewal planning?

For families of students with disabilities who are considering a charter school, the right questions are equally direct: What share of students currently have IEPs? How does co-teaching work in practice? Are related services like speech therapy or occupational therapy provided on-site, and what does continuity look like if a provider changes?

A Long-Term Equity Play

The research is consistent on one more point: participating schools in the study had the most impact when using a combination of strategies rather than a single solution. There is no single lever — inclusion is a culture, not a program.

The enrollment gap between charter schools and district schools for students with disabilities has been narrowing slowly. The charter sector's track record on innovation, community responsiveness, and institutional flexibility gives it genuine tools to accelerate that progress. But tools only work when leaders pick them up intentionally.

Charter schools exist, in part, because their founders believed every child deserves access to a great education. Closing the special education gap isn't a compliance exercise — it's a test of whether that belief extends to every child in the lottery pool, every family that shows up at the door with an IEP in hand.
