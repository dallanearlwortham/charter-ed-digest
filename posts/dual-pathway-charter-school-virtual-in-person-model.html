Same School, Two Pathways: The Dual-Format Charter Trend

When "In-Person or Virtual?" Becomes "Both"

For most of the charter school era, families faced a binary: enroll your child at a physical campus or seek out a separate online school. That binary is dissolving. A fast-growing number of established charter organizations now operate full virtual academies alongside their brick-and-mortar campuses — under the same mission, the same leadership, and often the same academic philosophy. It's one of the most consequential structural shifts in K-12 education right now, and families navigating their options need to understand what it actually means.

What the Dual-Pathway Charter Model Is — and Isn't

A dual-pathway charter school is an organization that holds authorization for at least one physical campus and a separate virtual or remote academy. Students and families can choose their learning format — and, in many cases, switch between them as circumstances change — without leaving the school they already trust.

This is different from a "hybrid schedule" school where every student attends some days in-person and some days remotely. In the dual-pathway model, the two programs typically serve distinct student bodies with separate daily structures. What they share is the core: curriculum values, accountability expectations, and school culture.

It's also different from a standalone virtual charter. When a brick-and-mortar charter operates its own virtual academy, families aren't starting over with an unknown provider — they're extending a relationship they already have.

Why This Trend Is Accelerating in 2025

Several forces converged to push charter organizations toward this model:

Post-pandemic demand that didn't fade. Many families who discovered online learning during school closures found that it genuinely worked better for their children. Charter organizations that added virtual options retained students who might otherwise have left for other online providers.

State policy opening new doors. North Carolina is a sharp illustration of the pace of change. After passing legislation in 2023 allowing traditional brick-and-mortar charters to establish separate online programs, the state went from 10 remote charter academies to 19 expected to operate in the 2025–26 school year — and the Charter Schools Review Board has since approved a total of 21 remote charter academies. That near-doubling happened in a single year. Other states are watching closely.

A market that's growing fast. The virtual charter schools market was valued at $5.62 billion in 2025 and is projected to reach $20.47 billion by 2035 — a compound annual growth rate of roughly 13.8%. Flexibility, personalized learning, improved access to quality teachers, and EdTech adoption are cited as the primary growth drivers.

Geographic reach without new buildings. A physical campus draws from a radius. A virtual academy can serve students anywhere in a state — reaching rural families, military families who move frequently, students with medical needs, or high schoolers with demanding schedules. Brick-and-mortar charters with long waitlists have used virtual academies to serve more students without the cost and timeline of constructing a new campus.

What Families Are Actually Searching For

Why do some families choose a virtual option within an existing charter school rather than a standalone online school?

The answer usually comes down to trust and continuity. Families who already know a charter school's academic philosophy, teaching quality, and culture don't want to start over. A virtual academy inside a trusted organization lets a student stay connected to the same community — siblings can remain in the same system, parents don't rebuild relationships from scratch, and the school's accountability record is already known. Add flexible scheduling, individualized pacing, and technology-enabled instruction, and you get a profile that serves a wide range of learners: students managing health conditions, athletes with intense training schedules, families pursuing travel, and kids who simply learn better with more control over their environment.

The Model in Practice: One School, Two Formats

What does a well-executed dual-pathway charter actually look like from the inside? The organizing principle is that both formats deliver the same core mission — not a watered-down version of it for the students who aren't on campus.

Freedom Prep — formally Freedom Preparatory Academy, a Utah charter serving students from kindergarten through 12th grade — is one example of an organization that has built this out across the full K–12 span. In addition to its physical elementary and secondary campuses, the school operates a K–12 Virtual Academy serving students throughout the state. Rather than treating the online program as a separate brand, Freedom Prep applies the same foundational approach across both formats: pairing academic achievement with character development, offering smaller class sizes and technology-enabled instruction, and giving high school students the ability to focus on one of five interest-based Academies to develop their individual strengths. A Utah family whose circumstances change — a move to a rural area, a student who needs a more flexible schedule, a high schooler pursuing a serious outside commitment — doesn't have to leave the school community to find a format that works.

That kind of continuity is exactly what distinguishes a true dual-pathway organization from one that simply licenses a third-party virtual curriculum and calls it a program.

What Separates Strong Dual-Pathway Programs from Weak Ones

Not every dual-pathway model is executed well. The accountability conversation is real: as North Carolina's Charter Schools Review Board debated the rapid expansion of virtual programs, one board member asked the pointed question that every authorizer — and every family — should be asking: "What does best practice look like for a virtual school?" It's the right question.

Here's what to evaluate when a charter offers both formats:

  • Shared academic coherence. Both pathways should reflect the same instructional standards and student support philosophy — not just the same logo. Ask whether the virtual curriculum is built in-house or outsourced to a third-party provider, and whether it aligns with what the physical campus delivers.
  • Dedicated virtual staff. Virtual students need their own support infrastructure: teachers experienced in asynchronous and synchronous online instruction, intervention specialists, and advisors who proactively monitor engagement rather than waiting for students to fall behind.
  • Disaggregated outcomes data. Request academic performance data specifically for the virtual cohort — graduation rates, state assessment proficiency, attendance — not just the organization's combined numbers.
  • Community connection. The best virtual programs within charter organizations don't just digitize schoolwork. Two-thirds of California's flex-based charter schools use hybrid models that blend virtual instruction with in-person learning, precisely because community connection matters to families even when the primary format is online.
  • Clear accountability terms. In states like North Carolina, remote charter academies operate under fixed-term contracts with separate renewal processes. Understand the authorization structure before enrolling.

What Charter Leaders Should Know Before Adding a Virtual Program

If your organization is considering adding a virtual academy, the operational lift is real. A virtual program requires a separate learning management system, purpose-built asynchronous curriculum, compliance structures that may differ from your physical campus, and staff trained for a different kind of student relationship. Rushing the build to capture enrollment funding — a concern state officials have raised publicly — produces programs that underserve students and ultimately damage the organization's reputation.

The charter organizations seeing success with the dual-pathway model built their virtual programs deliberately, with fidelity to the same mission that made their physical campuses worth attending. That deliberateness is the difference between a genuine expansion of access and a name-only extension that families eventually see through.

The families choosing dual-pathway charter schools aren't looking for an easier education. They're looking for one that fits their actual lives — and increasingly, the charter sector is building to meet them there.
