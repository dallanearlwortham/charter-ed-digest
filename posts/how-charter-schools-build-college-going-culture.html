How Charter Schools Build a College-Going Culture

# How the Best Charter Schools Build a College-Going Culture That Actually Works

Walk into one of the highest-performing charter schools in the country and something immediately stands out: college isn't treated as a distant possibility. It's the organizing premise of the whole institution—visible in hallway banners, embedded in advisory periods, woven into the language teachers use with ninth graders on the very first day of school. That's not accidental. It's strategy.

A growing body of research confirms that the college-going culture a school deliberately creates is one of the strongest levers it has for closing postsecondary gaps—especially for low-income students, students of color, and the first in their families to pursue a degree. For charter school leaders and for families choosing a school, understanding what that culture actually looks like in practice has never been more important.

What "College-Going Culture" Really Means

College-going culture is not the same thing as offering AP courses or posting college pennants outside the counselor's office. At its core, it refers to a school-wide, sustained set of expectations, structures, and relationships that make college attendance feel like a normal, supported, and fully achievable next step—for every student, not just the top academic performers.

Researchers and practitioners describe a genuine college-going culture as one that:

  • Sets a clear institutional identity around postsecondary access from the first day of enrollment
  • Embeds college and career preparation into the daily schedule—not siloed into a single elective or a senior-year crash course
  • Builds ongoing college awareness through campus visits, alumni speakers, and explicit conversations about majors, application timelines, and financial aid
  • Removes practical barriers such as test registration fees, application deadlines, and FAFSA confusion that disproportionately derail first-generation students
  • Tracks post-graduation data to learn which college placements actually lead to degree completion—and adjusts counseling and programming accordingly

That last point is increasingly separating good charter schools from great ones. Using tools like the National Student Clearinghouse's Student Tracker, the most forward-thinking charter networks are following their graduates onto college campuses, identifying where students struggle, and redesigning their high-school programs in response—years before problems would otherwise surface in graduation statistics.

What the Research Says

Does a school's college-going culture actually change student outcomes? The answer, supported by several rigorous studies, is yes—and the effects are substantial.

A lottery-based study of five high-performing charter high schools in Los Angeles found that enrolling in a charter school significantly increased both 11th-grade standardized test scores and four-year college enrollment and persistence. Charter students were more likely to take advanced coursework, less likely to skip class, more likely to apply to a four-year college, and—critically—reported greater teacher support for college-going as a distinct, measurable school characteristic. Enrollment and persistence in University of California campuses, which carry higher graduation rates than the likely alternatives for these students, rose by roughly 11 to 12 percentage points.

Stanford's CREDO national charter school study found that urban charter students gain the equivalent of 29 additional days of learning per year in reading and 28 in math compared to peers in traditional public schools. The compounding effect is even more striking: by year four of enrollment, charter students show the equivalent of 45 additional days of reading growth annually—suggesting that sustained immersion in a high-expectation environment produces results that short-term snapshots consistently understate.

A 2024 California Charter Schools Association report zeroed in on the specific practices distinguishing the highest-performing charter schools on college access. Researchers identified 135 California charter schools that consistently send a higher proportion of low-income, Black, and Latino graduates to four-year colleges than the state average—and California charter public schools overall are sending more than double the percentage of English learners and students with disabilities to UC and CSU campuses compared to the public school average. Among the very best performers, five practices appeared consistently: setting a clear institutional purpose around college and career, embedding preparation into the schedule and curriculum, continually building awareness of postsecondary options, removing access barriers, and tracking outcome data for continuous improvement.

What It Looks Like Inside a School

The schools doing this best share several observable characteristics that go well beyond policy statements and promotional materials.

Advisory and mentorship systems are structural, not supplemental. Rather than relying on one overextended college counselor for several hundred students, high-performing charter schools build college-going support into advisory structures, homerooms, and even subject-area classes—so that every adult in the building reinforces the same expectations and the same vocabulary around postsecondary access.

The commitment doesn't end at graduation. Several leading charter networks have expanded into dedicated alumni support, deploying college success advisors who follow graduates onto campuses. These advisors help students navigate financial aid crises, transfer processes, and the cultural disorientation that first-generation students frequently describe when they arrive at a four-year institution far from home. As Education Next has documented, these advisors help students select courses strategically, connect with campus resources they might not seek out on their own, and persist through the difficult stretches that derail too many otherwise capable students.

Data closes the feedback loop. The fastest-improving charter schools use college persistence data—not just enrollment data—to hold themselves accountable and redirect resources. If graduates are consistently struggling at a particular type of institution, those schools adjust their college-match counseling before the next cohort makes the same costly mistake.

Noble - Baker College Prep, a grades 9–12 campus within the Noble Schools network of 17 free, open-enrollment public charter schools in Chicago, puts this philosophy into plain language on its own website: the school describes its mission as providing rigorous college preparatory coursework so that students can "graduate from our nation's top ranked universities and colleges, and thereby strengthen the communities from which they come." That framing—connecting academic achievement to a larger sense of civic purpose and community investment—reflects exactly what researchers identify as a key protective factor for first-generation students: a reason to persist that is bigger than any single obstacle.

Questions Families Should Be Asking

For parents evaluating charter schools, published graduation and college acceptance rates are a starting point—but they don't tell the whole story. More revealing questions include:

  • What percentage of graduates enroll in four-year colleges, and what percentage are still enrolled two or three years later?
  • How is college preparation built into the daily experience of a 9th grader, not just a 12th grader?
  • Does the school track what happens to graduates after they leave—and does that data visibly shape what happens inside the building?
  • What specific support exists for students who are the first in their family to navigate the college application process?

Schools that have clear, specific, data-backed answers to those questions have almost certainly done the deeper cultural work as well. Vague answers—or a pivot straight to acceptance-rate brochures—are themselves informative.

The Bottom Line

A college-going culture isn't a marketing phrase or a coat of paint applied over a standard curriculum. The evidence shows it is a structural choice, made deliberately in scheduling, staffing, professional development, and data systems, that measurably changes where students end up and whether they stay once they get there. For charter school leaders building or strengthening a college-prep identity, and for families trying to identify schools with a genuine commitment to postsecondary success, the difference between talking about college and building an entire school around it has consequences that follow students for the rest of their lives.
