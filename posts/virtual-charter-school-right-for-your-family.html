Is a Virtual Charter School Right for Your Family?

Virtual Charter School Enrollment Is Still Growing—But Is It Right for Every Student?

Five years after the COVID-19 pandemic normalized screens-as-classrooms for millions of American kids, full-time virtual charter schools are not a pandemic relic. They are a permanent, expanding sector of K–12 public education—and families across the country are actively choosing them, not just tolerating them.

Charter school enrollment increased by nearly 493,000 students—almost 15 percent—nationwide between the 2019–2020 and 2024–2025 school years, according to preliminary data from the National Alliance for Public Charter Schools. Virtual options are a meaningful slice of that growth. In 42 states and Washington, D.C., online public schools have been established by state authorities, school districts, or charter schools, allowing students to attend tuition-free for all grades K–12.

But enrollment growth alone doesn't answer the most important question charter school leaders and families should be asking: Which students genuinely thrive in a fully virtual environment—and what should families look for before they enroll?

---

Why Families Are Choosing Full-Time Virtual Schools

The reasons families move to virtual charter schools have evolved well beyond "we tried it during COVID and it stuck." Researchers and enrollment data now point to a more varied picture.

Robin Lake, director of the Center on Reinventing Public Education, has observed that a number of parents and students were pushed into the virtual option during the pandemic, liked it, and chose to continue—and that many teachers found they genuinely valued the scheduling and location flexibility that virtual delivery allowed.

Lake also points out that universal voucher programs can now be applied toward virtual schools in many states, further broadening their appeal to families who might not have previously considered the option.

Common reasons families cite for choosing virtual charter schools include:

  • Schedule flexibility for student athletes, performers, or children with ongoing medical needs
  • Geographic barriers, including rural families facing long commutes to quality schools
  • Learning differences that benefit from a self-paced or more individualized environment
  • Advanced or early-graduation tracks that a traditional school can't easily accommodate
  • Family obligations, including students who work or help care for family members
  • Homeschool-adjacent preferences, where families want structured curriculum with public-school accountability

Jennifer Darling-Aduana, an assistant professor of learning technologies at Georgia State University, notes that within ten years she expects brick-and-mortar schools to remain the default—but that "there's a growing segment of the population seeing a benefit, whether it's academic or social or logistical."

---

What the Enrollment Data Actually Shows

Looking at some of the largest statewide virtual school programs confirms steady, real growth. Georgia Cyber Academy enrolled 9,966 students in 2024–2025, up from 9,762 the prior year. Texas's Lone Star Virtual Academy High School climbed from 6,793 students in 2021–2022 to 8,114 in the current academic year. Massachusetts's program—capped at 1,200 students—nearly doubled from 585 enrollees in 2018 to 1,184 this year. And in Michigan, 11 percent of all public school students took at least one virtual course, with 68 percent of the state's public school districts reporting at least one full-time virtual enrollment.

Across these states, virtual school enrollment skews toward high school students—which may actually benefit the broader system, since students who might otherwise have dropped out are opting instead to stay enrolled in a virtual option.

---

A Model That Blends Online and In-Person Support

One approach gaining traction is the hybrid-support model: fully online instruction delivered through a virtual charter school, paired with optional in-person support centers, extracurricular access, and enrichment programs. This structure attempts to solve the most common criticism of pure virtual schools—student isolation and the need for heavy parental oversight—without sacrificing the scheduling flexibility families value.

Lisa Academy (Arkansas Online School) is one example of this thinking put into practice. The Arkansas K–12 public charter school offers both live and flex learning options for students statewide, while also providing access to in-person learning centers in Central Arkansas and Northwest Arkansas for intervention, tutoring, and STEM-C enrichment. Students can even try out for sports teams at traditional LISA campuses—meaning a virtual enrollment doesn't have to mean trading away the full school experience.

---

What the Research Says—Honestly

Is a full-time virtual charter school as effective as in-person school? For most students, the honest answer from current research is: not automatically, and not without the right conditions.

The National Education Policy Center found that full-time virtual schools posted a four-year graduation rate of 65.1 percent—well below the national average of 86.5 percent. Virtual charter schools specifically came in at 59.4 percent, compared to 66.7 percent for district-operated virtual schools.

Boston University economist Joshua Goodman puts it plainly: "I think we have a growing body of evidence that virtual education is much trickier to pull off than in-person education for the vast majority of students. There are some for whom the virtual option makes sense, but in general it makes sense to start from a default assumption of skepticism with virtual schools and be very selective in which students we encourage to use such options."

Lake echoes the concern: it often requires a parent closely overseeing a child's education at home, and there are a limited number of families who can realistically do that. The students who do well tend to be highly self-motivated or have an unusually engaged parent—and that's a narrow slice of any school's potential enrollment pool.

---

How to Evaluate a Virtual Charter School Before You Enroll

Families considering a switch to full-time virtual should go beyond the school's homepage and ask pointed questions:

  1. What are the school's actual graduation and retention rates? Not every state publishes this data, so ask the school directly and compare against your state's averages.
  2. How much live, synchronous instruction is built into the schedule? A fully asynchronous program demands high self-direction from both student and parent.
  3. What does the school offer for students with IEPs or 504 plans? Virtual delivery of special education services varies widely in quality and compliance.
  4. Are there in-person touchpoints—learning centers, events, or extracurriculars? These matter significantly for social development and sustained engagement.
  5. Who built the curriculum—classroom teachers or a third-party EdTech vendor? Look for evidence-based instructional design with clear ties to teacher input.
  6. What parent involvement is realistically expected? An honest, specific answer here is one of the clearest signals of a trustworthy program.
  7. Can you speak with current families or students? Darling-Aduana recommends, similarly to choosing a college, that families connect with people who have real experience in the program and no vested interest in selling it.

---

What This Means for Charter School Leaders

Virtual charter schools are no longer a niche or a novelty—they're a durable part of the school choice landscape, and families are choosing them for real, valid reasons. But the outcome data is clear: results depend heavily on student fit, family capacity, and program quality.

For charter leaders, that creates both an opportunity and a responsibility. Design virtual programs with honest transparency about who they serve best. Build in the support structures—in-person options, synchronous touchpoints, robust advising—that the research shows matter most. And resist the temptation to treat online enrollment as a low-cost growth lever rather than a genuine educational commitment.

The families who thrive in virtual charter schools aren't looking for the easy path. They're looking for the right one—and the schools that help them find it will be the ones worth choosing.
