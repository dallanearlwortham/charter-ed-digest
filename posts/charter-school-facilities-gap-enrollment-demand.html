Charter School Demand Is Soaring. Buildings Are the Bottleneck.

# Charter School Demand Is Soaring. Buildings Are the Bottleneck.

More families than ever want a charter school seat for their child—and in state after state, there simply aren't enough of them. The problem isn't interest. It isn't policy. It's square footage.

According to the National Alliance for Public Charter Schools' 2025 Enrollment Brief, charter schools now serve nearly 4 million students across more than 8,100 schools nationwide—the highest enrollment figure ever recorded for the sector. Over the past six years, charters have added more than half a million students, a 15% increase, even as the overall school-age population has contracted and traditional district enrollment has fallen by more than 4%. The demand signal could not be clearer.

Yet in North Carolina—one of the most closely watched charter markets in the country—77% of charter schools reported waitlists in the most recent school year, with more than 74,000 students in line for a seat. The number of charter schools in that state actually declined for the first time in 14 years in 2024–25, not because interest dried up but because the obstacles to opening new schools had become formidable. The challenge those schools named most often? Facilities.

This is the paradox at the center of the charter school movement right now: surging family demand running headlong into a brick-and-mortar ceiling.

---

Why Facilities Are the Hardest Problem in Charter Growth

Why can't charter schools just find a building and open?

It sounds simple. It is not. Unlike district public schools, which are typically assigned existing facilities maintained with public bond funds and capital budgets, most charter schools must find, finance, and maintain their own physical space—often without access to the same public revenue streams that built the district school across the street.

The National Alliance for Public Charter Schools has identified facilities access as "one of the biggest barriers to charter school growth." The obstacles compound on one another:

  • No automatic capital funding. In most states, charter schools receive per-pupil operating dollars but no dedicated capital allotment. A building that costs $5–$20 million to purchase or build must be financed through other means—philanthropic capital, charter-specific loan products, or creative lease arrangements.
  • Limited credit history. New charter schools often lack the track record that traditional lenders require, making commercial financing difficult to access even when a school has strong enrollment projections.
  • District facility inequity. In states where districts control bond revenue, charter schools—even those serving large percentages of district students—are frequently excluded from the capital planning process. The money flows to district-operated schools by default.
  • Rising construction and lease costs. Post-pandemic real estate markets have pushed commercial and institutional property costs sharply higher in many urban and suburban markets, exactly where charter demand tends to concentrate.

The result: capable charter operators with proven models, eager families, and full waitlists are stalled not by educational failure but by real estate economics.

---

What's Shifting at the Federal and State Level

The good news is that the facilities problem is finally getting sustained policy attention—from Washington and from state capitals.

At the federal level, the Department of Education awarded seven new grants in September 2025 under the Charter Schools Program for opening, replicating, and expanding high-quality charter schools. In May 2025, the U.S. Department of Education also announced a $60 million funding increase for the Charter Schools Program overall. Separately, a bipartisan proposal in the Senate—the High-Quality Charter Schools Act—would create a tax credit equal to 75% of qualified charitable contributions to eligible nonprofit charter organizations, aiming to unlock private capital for sector growth.

On the philanthropic side, the Walton Family Foundation, PNC Bank, and Civic Builders announced a $100 million expansion of the Facilities Investment Fund in May 2025, offering up to 100% financing for school facilities specifically for charter schools—a significant move aimed at removing the financing gap that stops growth-ready schools cold.

At the state level, a growing number of legislatures are moving toward per-pupil facilities allotments. Advocates point out that states ranging from Colorado and Illinois to Massachusetts have adopted or expanded these allotments, and that access to such funding unlocks tens of millions of additional federal dollars.

Colorado is a case in point. The Colorado League of Charter Schools—a statewide organization that supports Colorado's charter schools through advocacy, events, resources, and school services—spent considerable energy pushing for exactly this kind of structural remedy. In May 2026, Governor Polis signed Senate Bill 145 into law, a measure requiring school districts to include charter schools in bond planning processes, notify them of whether their capital needs will be addressed, and post detailed public information about which schools benefit from bond revenue. The law is a direct response to a striking disparity: Colorado charter schools serve more than 15% of the state's public school students yet had received roughly 3% of the more than $15 billion in district bond revenue collected over the preceding decade.

What Charter Leaders Can Do Right Now

Policy momentum is real, but it moves slowly. Charter operators facing a waitlist and a facilities constraint cannot wait for the next legislative session. Here are the most actionable near-term moves:

Explore federal CSP grant eligibility. The Charter Schools Program offers grants specifically for replication and expansion. If your school has a track record of strong outcomes, you may qualify. The application process is competitive but the awards are substantial.

Pursue state per-pupil facilities allotments where available. If your state has enacted a facilities allotment, ensure you're accessing it fully and factoring it into your capital plan. If your state has not, document the disparity—that data is your most powerful advocacy tool.

Engage charter-specific lenders and CDFIs. Community development financial institutions and specialized charter school lenders (including those participating in funds like the Walton/Civic Builders facility program) often have more flexible underwriting criteria than conventional banks and exist precisely to serve this gap.

Join or form facility-sharing networks. In urban markets with multiple charter operators, shared-use agreements for gymnasiums, performing arts spaces, or administrative facilities can stretch each school's footprint without proportional cost increases.

Document your waitlist publicly and politically. Families on waiting lists represent the most powerful political constituency for charter facility reform. Making that number visible—in local media, in legislative testimony, in your school's annual report—keeps the pressure where it belongs.

---

The Bigger Picture

The enrollment data tells a story of genuine, durable family preference for charter schools—a preference that persists even as ESA programs expand, private school choice grows, and new virtual options multiply. Families are choosing charters not by default but by conviction. The sector's challenge is no longer proving that demand exists. It is building fast enough to meet it.

In state after state, the constraint on charter growth is not educational will—it is physical space. Solving that problem requires simultaneous action at multiple levels: federal grant programs, state legislative reform, philanthropic capital deployment, and savvy financial planning by individual operators. All of those pieces are moving right now, faster than at any point in the past decade.

The families on those waiting lists are watching to see whether the sector can build its way to them.
