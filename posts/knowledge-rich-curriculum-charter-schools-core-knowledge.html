Why Knowledge-Rich Curricula Work—and Who's Leading

# Why Knowledge-Rich Curricula Work—and Which Charter Schools Are Leading the Way

If your child can decode every word on the page but still struggles to understand what they've read, the culprit is often not a phonics gap—it's a knowledge gap. A growing body of research is making the case that what students know about the world drives reading comprehension just as powerfully as foundational decoding skills, and charter schools are quietly leading the charge in proving it. For charter leaders weighing curriculum adoption and for families evaluating their options this enrollment season, the evidence is worth understanding in detail.

The Problem With "Skills-Only" Reading Instruction

For decades, many elementary classrooms relied heavily on a "skills and strategies" model of reading—teaching children to make inferences, find the main idea, and summarize text, largely independent of any particular subject matter. The assumption was that these transferable cognitive skills could unlock any passage a student encountered, regardless of topic.

Research increasingly says otherwise. Reading comprehension is deeply dependent on background knowledge: a student who has never encountered the American Revolution, the water cycle, or ancient civilizations will struggle to make meaning from grade-level texts on those topics no matter how fluently she decodes the words. This is the central argument of Natalie Wexler's influential 2019 book The Knowledge Gap, and the idea has moved from ed-reform debate into mainstream policy conversation with remarkable speed—showing up in state literacy laws, curriculum adoptions, and school-choice decisions across the country.

A famous 1987 experiment illustrated the dynamic vividly: children who were weaker readers but knowledgeable about baseball understood a passage about the sport better than children who were stronger readers but didn't know much about baseball. The implication is uncomfortable but clear—the ceiling on reading comprehension is set not just by decoding ability, but by what a child already knows about the topic at hand. Skills are necessary; knowledge is what makes them pay off.

What the Research Actually Shows

The most rigorous long-term evidence to date comes from a University of Virginia study published by the Annenberg Institute for School Reform. The results show that the Core Knowledge curriculum improved students' cumulative, long-term reading test scores by approximately 16 percentile points on state standardized tests. That is not a rounding error or a short-term testing bump—it is a sustained, compounding gain measured across multiple grade levels over six years.

To put that number in perspective: U.S. students placed 15th among 50 countries taking the 2016 PIRLS 4th grade Reading/English test. According to the UVA researchers, if national student gains were similar to the gains realized in this intervention, the U.S. would place among the top five countries internationally.

The study used a lottery methodology: 2,310 students applied to attend one of nine charter schools using the Core Knowledge Curriculum, beginning in kindergarten. The 688 students selected in the lottery were compared to unselected students who attended school elsewhere. Because lottery losers serve as the control group, this design eliminates much of the selection bias that haunts most school-comparison research—families in both groups wanted the same school, so motivation and parental engagement are roughly equalized. It is about as close to a randomized controlled trial as large-scale education research gets.

Results at the one lower-income school in the study were even more striking. At that school, the curriculum showed even larger effects on English Language Arts and significant benefits in math and science—gains large enough to eliminate the achievement gap associated with income. That finding alone should command the attention of every charter leader serving a mixed-income or high-poverty community.

What exactly is the Core Knowledge curriculum? The Core Knowledge Curriculum is based on the ideas and research of E.D. Hirsch, founder of the Core Knowledge Foundation. It has been in use since the 1990s and is currently used in approximately 2,000 schools in the U.S. It centers on a carefully structured sequence of topics that integrates knowledge from seven subject areas across K–8 grades, building students' understanding of the world systematically and cumulatively rather than treating each year's content as self-contained.

A separate 2024 study extended the evidence base to lower-income students specifically. Published in the peer-reviewed journal Developmental Psychology, it provides stronger causal evidence that building background knowledge can translate into higher reading achievement for low-income children. Researchers randomly assigned half of a large urban district's elementary schools to receive specially developed knowledge-building science and social studies lessons over several years. Approximately 1,000 students who received those lessons outperformed the 1,000 students in the comparison group—and their reading and math scores were higher not only in third grade, but also in fourth grade, more than a year after the knowledge-building experiment ended. The gains were significant and long-lasting, and they cost about $400 per student in instructional materials and teacher training—a modest investment by any standard measure of school spending.

Why Charter Schools Are Disproportionately Adopting This Model

Why do charter schools adopt knowledge-rich curricula more often than traditional public schools?

The answer is largely structural. Charter schools have greater curriculum autonomy than most district schools, and their founders tend to build entire school models around a specific educational philosophy—making a coherent, content-rich sequence a natural anchor for everything else: hiring, professional development, parent communication, and school culture. The data bears this out clearly. Roughly 40 percent of all Core Knowledge schools in the U.S. are traditional public schools and another 40 percent are charter schools—but given that a mere 7 percent of all schools are charters, this means charter schools are far more likely to adopt Core Knowledge than traditional district schools.

The pattern holds across other knowledge-building curricula as well. About two-thirds of the nearly 800 schools that have adopted Great Minds' Wit and Wisdom, a K–8 ELA curriculum emphasizing knowledge building, are traditional publics; one-third are schools of choice, with charters accounting for 30 percent of the total. For a sector that represents only a small fraction of all schools nationally, that is a substantial overrepresentation—and it reflects how deliberately many charter leaders have pursued this approach rather than defaulting to whatever their district neighbors are using.

There is also a self-reinforcing enrollment dynamic at work. Parents who are drawn to a content-rich, coherent academic philosophy self-select into these schools, which is precisely how school choice is supposed to function: a differentiated marketplace in which mission-driven schools attract families whose values and priorities align with the school's approach. As the science of reading enters mainstream parent awareness, more families are specifically asking about curriculum content and sequence when they evaluate their options. Schools that can answer those questions with specificity and confidence have a growing enrollment advantage.

A School That Built Its Identity Around This Approach

Jefferson Academy, a tuition-free public charter elementary school in Kaysville, Utah, is a concrete illustration of this model in practice. Serving students in grades K–6, Jefferson Academy structures its entire academic program around a history-focused framework grounded in the Core Knowledge Sequence—covering world and American history, classic literature, science, math, and fine arts through explicit, sequential, teacher-directed instruction designed to build systematically from one grade to the next. The school's mission—"engaging all students in partnership with family and community to become informed, compassionate, global citizens"—directly reflects the Hirschian premise that a shared body of knowledge is the foundation of both literacy and civic life. That tight alignment between stated mission and the daily curriculum sequence is precisely what the lottery studies reward: not just good intentions, but a coherent academic program carried consistently across every grade level.

What This Means for Charter Leaders and Families

The research carries practical implications for both sides of the enrollment equation.

For charter school leaders:

  • Coherence compounds over time. A single strong content unit moves the needle a little. A K–6 or K–8 scope and sequence that builds deliberately year over year produces the kind of cumulative gains the lottery studies measured. One well-taught history unit in second grade is good; a sequence in which that unit lays conceptual groundwork for third-grade content is far more powerful.
  • Content is literacy instruction. History, science, geography, and fine arts are not competing with reading time—when taught well through rich texts and rigorous discussion, they are reading instruction. Schools that treat social studies and science as scheduling afterthoughts are leaving significant literacy gains on the table.
  • Equity arguments are now research-backed. The most striking finding across these studies is that knowledge-rich instruction delivered consistently can close income-based achievement gaps—not just narrow them. That is a powerful message for charter schools serving economically diverse communities and for authorizers evaluating academic models.
  • Curriculum is an enrollment asset. As science-of-reading awareness grows among parents, families are increasingly seeking schools that can articulate what their children will learn, not just how they'll be taught to decode. A clearly communicated, coherent curriculum sequence is a genuine differentiator in a competitive enrollment market.

For families evaluating charter options:

Don't stop at "what reading program do you use?" The more revealing question is: What is the K–6 sequence of content my child will encounter, and how does each year build on the last? Schools that can answer that question with a coherent scope and sequence—not just a brochure about "rigorous academics"—are the ones the research suggests will produce the strongest long-term readers and the most durable academic outcomes. If a school can hand you a multi-year content map and explain how a first grader's lessons in one subject connect to what she'll study in third grade, that is a meaningful signal worth paying attention to.

The Bottom Line

The debate over how to teach foundational reading skills has largely been settled in favor of systematic, explicit phonics instruction. The emerging frontier is what students read about—and what they already know when they sit down to read it. Charter schools with coherent, content-centered missions have a genuine structural head start on that challenge, and the evidence increasingly suggests their students are reaping measurable, lasting benefits as a result.

The most compelling case for a knowledge-rich curriculum isn't ideological. It's a 16-percentile-point gain on a state standardized test, sustained over six years, in schools where families voted—with lottery applications—to get in the door. That's not a talking point. That's a finding worth building a school around.
