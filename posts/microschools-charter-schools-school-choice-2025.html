Microschools Are Rising. What Charter Schools Should Do Now

The School Choice Map Just Got a Lot More Complicated

For most of the charter school era, the competitive landscape was relatively simple: charter schools competed with traditional district schools for students and funding. That map has been redrawn.

In a national survey conducted in June 2025, 74% of American parents with school-aged children said they considered sending at least one of their kids to a different school in the last 12 months. That is around 48 million parents actively thinking about their options. But here is the detail that should catch every charter leader's attention: families are not just considering charters. They are weighing microschools, hybrid homeschools, learning pods, and full-time online programs—a sprawling new ecosystem of alternatives that did not exist at meaningful scale five years ago.

Charter schools are still winning plenty of those decisions. Public charter schools are the only type of public school growing in enrollment in 2025, with about 7,800 charter schools in the U.S. serving about 3.7 million children. But the ground beneath them is shifting fast, and leaders who understand the microschool movement will be better positioned to respond—whether that means competing, collaborating, or simply borrowing the best ideas from it.

What Exactly Is a Microschool?

Microschools are small, often parent- or teacher-founded learning environments that prioritize personalization, multiage groupings, and flexibility over the rigid structures of a traditional school day. Popularity grew during the pandemic when families sought learning alternatives in online, hybrid, and pod formats; an estimated 750,000 to 2 million students now attend them nationwide. The schools are typically privately run, but a growing number of public school and charter leaders are now seeing a clear role for them within their own systems.

Charter schools, private schools, microschools, and homeschool pods are all drawing families in with promises of flexibility, personalization, and smaller environments. Homeschooling doubled in the U.S. between 2020 and 2023. A 2024 survey found that 37% of parents are more interested in microschools or hybrid options than they were before the pandemic.

That is not a fringe movement. That is a structural shift in how millions of families think about school.

Why Families Are Being Drawn Away

What draws families to microschools instead of charter schools? The honest answer is that microschools offer something many charters have not yet cracked: the feeling that the school was built specifically for their child, not for a general population of students. Small group sizes, flexible daily schedules, and learning that happens partly outside classroom walls are the core appeals.

Conversations with parents who chose homeschooling or microschools made clear that they were not necessarily dissatisfied with public education itself—they were seeking alternatives that offered more flexibility, individuality, and personalized learning. District leaders who examined the trend closely found that it pointed to the value of starting fresh, without being locked into rigid bell schedules, one-size-fits-all lessons, and a system where students advance based on age and seat time rather than demonstrated mastery.

That framing should feel familiar to anyone who has ever read an original charter school mission statement. It is almost exactly the argument that launched the charter movement in the first place.

The Competitive Reality Charter Leaders Cannot Ignore

Only about 5% of the country's microschools are public charter schools. But the National Microschooling Center reports hearing from public school superintendents in states with school choice who are growing curious about the model—because they are losing some of their best teachers and families to microschools and want to get out in front of it.

A 2025 analysis of more than 800 microschools found that more than 40% of enrolled students previously attended district-operated schools or were homeschooled before making the switch. That means microschools are drawing from the same restless family population that has historically been charter schools' most receptive audience: people actively seeking something better rather than passively accepting a default assignment.

The competitive threat is real. But so is the opportunity for charter schools willing to think clearly about their own identity and purpose. One school that illustrates what focused identity looks like in practice is Career Path High, a tuition-free charter school serving grades 9 through 12 in Kaysville, Utah. In an era when families are spoiled for choice, a high school whose very name telegraphs a precise destination—and whose mission is built squarely around it—deploys exactly the kind of sharp institutional clarity that microschools have long used as a recruitment advantage. A charter school does not have to be tiny to compete on identity. It just has to be honest and specific about what it is for.

Three Strategic Moves Worth Making Now

Charter leaders do not have to stand still while the landscape shifts. Here are three approaches worth examining seriously heading into the 2025-26 school year and beyond.

1. Borrow microschool design elements without abandoning charter accountability.

Education providers are innovating with models that combine small, in-person learning pods with flexible online curricula. Many states are also moving toward part-time enrollment policies that allow homeschoolers to access specific public school offerings—a trend researchers describe as the "unbundling" of education, expanding families' ability to mix and match. Charter schools can adopt these design concepts—smaller learning cohorts, flexible scheduling, project-based and community-connected work—while retaining the public accountability and tuition-free access that private microschools often cannot provide. The goal is not to become a microschool. It is to absorb the design principles that make microschools appealing and deliver them at charter scale.

2. Consider launching a charter microschool or charter-microschool hybrid.

In Indiana, a rural school district leader won approval from the state's charter school board to establish the Indiana Microschool Collaborative, designed to incubate a network of microschools statewide. These schools operate as charter schools—public, but with more flexibility in curricula and operations than traditional public schools. The first school in the network, Nature's Gift, received so many applications for its original 50 spots that it twice expanded capacity and still maintains a waiting list. Word has spread: the founder reports receiving inquiries from school district leaders and education organizations across the state and beyond. The lesson is straightforward: families want this model, and the charter framework can deliver it with public-school protections intact.

3. Compete aggressively on what microschools structurally cannot offer.

When parents who considered switching schools actually followed through, nearly 60% selected a district, public charter, or magnet school. The share who enrolled in private-sector options—microschools included—remained relatively low, due in large part to cost. Even with the expansion of education savings accounts and other private school choice programs, tuition-free public charter schools hold a structural pricing advantage that is very difficult for private microschools to overcome. Add in transparent academic accountability, legally guaranteed special education services, access to sports and extracurriculars, and federally protected civil rights obligations, and the charter value proposition is genuinely strong. The problem is rarely the product. Too often it is the clarity and consistency of the pitch.

Unbundling Is Already Happening Whether You Engage or Not

The deeper trend underneath all of this is the unbundling of school itself. Families increasingly refuse to assume that a single institution will satisfy every one of their children's academic, social, and developmental needs. They mix and match—a charter for core academics, a community co-op for arts enrichment, an online platform for advanced coursework, a local employer partnership for mentorship and career exposure.

Across the country, parents and teachers are launching new schools rather than waiting for traditional institutions to change. Charter schools were themselves born from that same impatience with the status quo. The leaders who remember that founding energy—and who build schools with the purposefulness and family-centeredness that originally defined the best charters—are the ones who will continue earning enrollment in an increasingly crowded and competitive field.

The Bottom Line

Microschools are not a pandemic hangover. They are not an education fad. They are a clear signal about what a growing and vocal share of families want: something smaller, more personal, more flexible, and more honestly connected to a purpose beyond seat time and standardized test scores. Charter schools have grown by more than 1.6 million students since 2000. But that growth was never inevitable, and it will not continue on autopilot.

The families are out there, actively looking. The question every charter leader should be sitting with right now is a simple one: does our school give those families a specific, compelling, and credible enough reason to choose us over a field of alternatives that is only going to keep growing?
