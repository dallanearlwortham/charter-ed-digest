Charter Enrollment Rises as Public School Rolls Shrink

# Charter Enrollment Is Rising While Overall School Rolls Shrink — What the 2025 Data Tells Us

Something significant is happening across American K-12 education right now: overall public school enrollment is declining in state after state, yet charter school rolls keep climbing. That gap is not a coincidence or a demographic quirk. It reflects a deliberate shift in how families are thinking about — and acting on — their educational options.

The 2025 numbers are fresh, specific, and worth understanding whether you lead a charter school or are a family weighing your options.

The National Picture: School Choice Is No Longer the Exception

As of July 2025, approximately 1,300,905 students are using private school choice programs nationwide — a 25% increase over the prior year, when that figure first surpassed one million for the first time. That surge is happening against a backdrop of broad parental restlessness. A January 2024 survey by the National School Choice Awareness Foundation found that 72% of parents considered a new school for their children in the past year, up from 52% in 2022 — a 35% relative increase in just two years.

Even with positive sentiment ticking upward in 2025, 59% of school parents still feel that K-12 education is on the wrong track in America — the third highest level of pessimism recorded across thirteen years of EdChoice's annual Schooling in America survey. That dissatisfaction is translating directly into enrollment decisions. Parents who are unhappy with the status quo are not simply complaining. They are moving their children.

Utah's Enrollment Data Is Especially Striking

Utah offers one of the clearest illustrations of this divergence anywhere in the country. Following a decade of steady growth, Utah's K-12 public school enrollment has declined for the third consecutive year. According to data released by the Utah State Board of Education, 656,311 students were enrolled in the public education system in the 2025-26 school year — a decrease of 11,478 students, or 1.7%, from the prior year. Prior to 2020, the state's enrollment hadn't declined since 2000.

Charter schools in the state, however, are moving in the opposite direction. Charter school enrollment grew by 3.6% and now represents 13% of Utah's total student population, with 49 charter schools experiencing at least a 1% increase in enrollment.

Utah's Superintendent of Public Instruction described the dynamic this way: "Utah's enrollment trends mirror the broader demographic shifts we're seeing nationwide — smaller birth cohorts, slowing in-migration, and increased school choice." Demographic headwinds are real — but they do not fully explain the charter growth. Families are not merely moving away from traditional district schools because of population shifts. They are actively moving toward specific schools because of what those schools offer.

What Are Families Actually Looking For?

What makes a family choose a charter school over their assigned district school? The research points to several consistent motivators. According to EdChoice's 2024 Schooling in America survey, school safety is now the top reason to choose a school among charter parents, cited by 37%. But safety is effectively table stakes. Beyond it, families consistently cite academic rigor, a clear and coherent curriculum, defined values, a smaller community feel, and meaningful two-way communication with teachers.

Waitlists across Utah charter schools continue to grow each year, and parents say they are choosing charters not because they are trendy, but because they offer learning environments where their children thrive — places with more intentional structures, more accessible teachers, and families who are invested in the same educational vision.

That last point carries real strategic weight. Families who apply to a charter school — often entering a waitlist lottery and filling out an application — are signaling a qualitatively different level of commitment than families who enroll in a neighborhood school by default. Charter schools that understand this, and build their culture and communication to honor that commitment, tend to retain families year over year and generate the word-of-mouth that keeps waitlists full.

Mission Clarity as a Competitive Enrollment Advantage

The charter schools gaining ground in this environment share a common trait: they are not generically "good schools." They are schools with a clear, specific identity that resonates with a defined community of families. When a school can answer the question "what do you stand for?" in one sentence — and then actually deliver on that promise consistently across every grade level — it becomes far easier for families to self-select in, stay enrolled, and refer their neighbors.

Jefferson Academy, a tuition-free public charter elementary school serving grades K–6 in Kaysville, Utah, is a concrete example of this kind of mission clarity in practice. The school's stated purpose is to engage "all students in partnership with family and community to become informed, compassionate, global citizens," and that mission is backed by a history-centered, content-rich curriculum built on the Core Knowledge Sequence — one that is explicit, sequential, and deliberately designed to build on itself from one grade to the next. In Davis County, where both district and charter schools are competing for an enrollment pool that is no longer growing, a school with that degree of curricular coherence and community-oriented identity carries a meaningful differentiating advantage that is difficult for a large district school to replicate.

What the Achievement Research Says

Families choosing charter schools are not only voting with their feet out of frustration — they are increasingly voting toward demonstrated academic results. A June 2023 study by the Center for Research on Education Outcomes (CREDO) at Stanford University estimated that students who attended charter schools advanced their learning in math by an additional six days in a year's time, and in reading added sixteen days of learning, compared to their traditional public school counterparts.

Those gains compound across years. A student who attends a high-quality charter from kindergarten through sixth or eighth grade accumulates a real and measurable academic advantage. Families who are paying attention to this research — and more of them are — factor it into their school selection decisions alongside factors like culture, safety, and communication.

What Charter Leaders Should Take Away Right Now

The enrollment trends of 2025 are sending a clear signal: the families most likely to seek out a charter school are engaged, motivated, and doing their research before they ever set foot at an open house. They are comparing options, reading mission statements carefully, and asking pointed questions about curriculum scope and sequence. For charter school leaders, this moment is both an opportunity and a responsibility.

A few practical implications worth acting on:

  • Articulate your mission with specificity. Vague promises of "excellence" do not differentiate you in a competitive enrollment market. A defined educational philosophy — something families can actually picture their child experiencing daily — does.
  • Make your curriculum visible and legible. Families who understand what their child will learn each year, and why the sequence is designed the way it is, become your most effective enrollment ambassadors.
  • Invest in community infrastructure. The most durable enrollment tool a charter school has is a satisfied family who tells three neighbors about their experience.
  • Watch your waitlist as a leading indicator. Waitlist growth often precedes enrollment growth by one to two years and is one of the most honest signals of brand strength in a local community.
  • Communicate proactively, not reactively. Families who chose you deliberately expect to be kept in the loop. Schools that meet that expectation build the loyalty that sustains enrollment even through demographic headwinds.

The families actively looking for an alternative to their default district school are out there in growing numbers, and the 2025 data makes clear their ranks are expanding every year. The schools that will capture and keep them are not necessarily the newest or the most innovative. They are the ones that are clear about who they are, consistent in what they deliver, and genuinely intentional about the community they build around learning.

That is what the enrollment data is really pointing toward — and it is a signal worth heeding now, before the charter school down the road gets there first.
