Why Charter Schools Struggle to Keep Great Teachers

# Why Charter Schools Struggle to Keep Great Teachers—And What Leaders Can Do About It

Charter school enrollment is at record highs and family demand keeps climbing. But quietly, in the background of that growth story, a stubborn operational problem is draining resources and undermining gains: charter schools lose teachers at a disproportionately high rate, and many of the standard fixes don't work as well as leaders hope.

Understanding why charter teacher turnover runs high—and which retention strategies actually move the needle—is one of the most consequential challenges facing school leaders right now.

The Numbers Are Worse Than Most Leaders Realize

Turnover rates are higher in charter schools (17.8%) than in traditional public schools (14.9%), according to a major Learning Policy Institute analysis of national teacher workforce data. That nearly three-point gap doesn't sound dramatic until you translate it into operational reality: more new hires to train, more institutional knowledge walking out the door, more time diverted from students.

Nearly 1 in 7 public school teachers moved schools or left the profession entirely between the 2020–21 and 2021–22 school years, and most of that turnover—74%—was voluntary and for reasons other than retirement. For charter schools, the picture is even more acute because of who tends to fill those classrooms. In the United States, a third of charter teachers have fewer than three years of teaching experience, compared to only about a fifth of public school teachers. That gap matters enormously for student outcomes: more than any other school-related factor, a teacher's efficacy matters most to student learning—and especially in the early years, nothing improves a teacher's efficacy quite like accumulated classroom experience.

The financial toll compounds the instructional cost. Replacing a teacher costs an estimated $25,000 in large districts and $12,000 in small districts on average (in 2024 dollars), diverting resources from investments that could better support students and educators. For a charter school that loses ten teachers in a single year, that is a potential six-figure drain before a single new hire has taught their first lesson.

What's Actually Driving Charter Teacher Turnover

Charter schools face retention pressures that are both structural and cultural—and they tend to compound each other in ways that are easy to misdiagnose.

On the culture side, many charter school teachers are initially drawn to the sector because charter schools are considered "elite positions" within a profession that often lacks prestige. These schools intentionally recruit driven, high-performing candidates by appealing to their desire for intellectual challenge and mission-driven work. Despite those high ideals, burnout quickly takes over. Working 60- to 80-hour weeks is manageable for a single, childless adult—but many teachers begin to question whether charter work is sustainable after typical adult milestones like marriage, children, or aging parents enter the picture.

The talent pipeline itself is also shrinking. The industry has experienced a 19% decrease in professionals earning education bachelor's degrees, meaning hiring timelines are longer and existing staff are left to absorb unfilled responsibilities. That added workload, layered on top of core teaching duties, accelerates burnout—which 59% of teachers report experiencing. From there, the math turns grim: 55% of educators end up leaving the profession earlier than they originally planned.

On the structural side, compensation is a genuine and specific problem for charter schools. Where pay differences don't dramatically affect teacher turnover in traditional public school settings, charter teachers typically receive lower compensation than their district counterparts—and colleagues often switch to traditional public schools for this very reason as they age and take on more financial responsibilities. On average, teachers already earn about 26% less than similarly educated workers elsewhere in the U.S. economy; charter teachers frequently earn less still.

Three Factors That Most Strongly Predict Whether Teachers Stay

The Learning Policy Institute's national analysis identified three variables that, after controlling for teacher and school characteristics, are most strongly associated with whether a teacher stays put or walks:

Compensation. With every $1,000 increase in salary adjusted for cost of living, the probability of turnover decreased by about 0.34 percentage points. Incremental raises add up over time.

School leadership. This is the most striking finding in the data: turnover rates were cut nearly in half when comparing teachers who reported low versus high levels of leadership effectiveness and support—18.7% turnover versus 9.0%. A principal making concrete improvements in how they support and communicate with staff can have a measurable, near-immediate impact on how many teachers return the following fall.

Job and workplace satisfaction. Teachers with high job and workplace satisfaction were significantly less likely to leave their schools than teachers with low satisfaction—8.0% turnover versus 22.0%. That is nearly a three-fold difference, driven substantially by working conditions and culture rather than compensation alone.

These three variables point toward a clear strategic conclusion: retention is a systems problem that requires simultaneous investment in pay, leadership development, and workplace quality. Pulling just one lever rarely produces lasting results.

What Retention Strategies Actually Work in Charter Schools?

Competitive Compensation—Necessary but Not Sufficient

Low pay is the top reason educators say they consider quitting, and teachers who receive meaningful raises are significantly less likely to leave. But compensation alone rarely closes a retention gap. True retention relies on comprehensive support—including mentorship, respect, flexibility, and purposeful work environments. Given that turnover costs up to $25,000 per departing teacher, even modest salary increases combined with cultural investments typically pay for themselves within a single budget cycle.

Structured Mentoring and Early-Career Support

Keeping educators on staff long-term helps schools maintain continuity, manage workloads more effectively, and free up time to build sustainable learning environments for students. The data on mentoring is particularly strong: 84% of districts prioritizing coaching and mentoring reported higher retention, and structured induction and professional development during the first five years of a teacher's career yield meaningfully longer tenure. For charter schools, this means building development programs that reflect the school's actual instructional model and culture—not recycled generic training—and pairing newer hires with experienced educators who understand the specific demands of charter school work.

Protecting Principal Time for People, Not Paperwork

Admin work often consumes 40–50% of charter school leaders' time. When principals are buried in HR tasks, compliance documentation, and operational logistics, they lose the bandwidth for teacher mentorship and culture-building that research identifies as the single most powerful driver of retention. Streamlining HR administration—whether through technology, outsourcing, or cleaner internal systems—frees leaders to do the work that actually keeps teachers in the building. The goal is straightforward: return principals to their role as instructional and cultural leaders rather than full-time administrators.

This is also where statewide policy advocacy intersects directly with what individual charter schools can afford on a day-to-day basis. The Texas Public Charter Schools Association, the Austin-based statewide organization whose mission is to advance a policy and regulatory climate that ensures every student in Texas has access to ever-improving public school options, works to reduce the compliance burdens and funding inequities that leave charter operators too stretched to invest adequately in their people.

Career Pathways That Give Great Teachers a Reason to Stay

One underappreciated reason experienced educators leave charter schools is that staying in the classroom can feel like a career plateau. Developing hybrid teacher-leader roles—where highly effective educators split their time between classroom instruction and coaching newer colleagues—addresses the burnout problem and the succession problem simultaneously. Experienced teachers stay engaged and continue growing professionally; early-career teachers get the embedded mentorship that research shows meaningfully improves retention in years one through five.

Workload Design and Wellbeing

Forty-three percent of teachers say they would stay in their current school if their workload improved. That is a large and actionable number. When high turnover occurs alongside a declining pipeline of new teachers entering the profession, schools face staffing challenges that leave remaining teachers overworked, with less time for planning, collaboration, and the student relationships that make the job meaningful. Structural interventions—adding teaching assistants to handle rote tasks, protecting dedicated planning periods during the school day, building mental health support explicitly into school culture—reduce the daily friction that pushes good teachers toward the exit. These are also, notably, investments in instructional quality: a teacher who is not overwhelmed is a more effective teacher.

What Is the Most Effective Teacher Retention Strategy for Charter Schools?

The most effective approach combines competitive compensation with strong school leadership, structured early-career mentoring, and deliberate investment in working conditions. No single policy works in isolation. Schools that address all three of the Learning Policy Institute's key predictors—pay, leadership quality, and workplace satisfaction—consistently see lower turnover than those that pull only one lever. The most underappreciated finding in the research remains the leadership result: improving how principals support and develop their teachers is associated with cutting turnover rates nearly in half. For a charter school operating on a tight budget with limited ability to dramatically increase salaries overnight, investing in principal development and leadership practice may be the single highest-ROI retention move available.

A Practical Starting Point for Charter Leaders

For leaders ready to move from awareness to action, a focused checklist looks like this:

  • Audit your turnover data by tenure, subject area, and teacher demographics. Early-career attrition and subject-specific shortages often tell different stories and call for different interventions.
  • Benchmark your compensation against district schools and other regional employers competing for the same candidates—not just other charter schools.
  • Build a structured induction program with assigned mentors, scheduled check-ins, and a first-year plan that integrates new teachers into school culture, not just procedures.
  • Create teacher-leader roles that let experienced educators advance professionally without leaving the classroom or the school.
  • Protect principal time for instructional leadership and direct teacher engagement. The data on leadership's impact is too strong to treat as a secondary priority.
  • Survey staff annually with specific questions about workload, autonomy, and administrative support—and make both the responses and your resulting actions visible to the full staff.

Teacher retention is not a soft culture issue. It is a core financial and mission priority. Every teacher who walks out the door takes institutional knowledge, student relationships, and real dollars with them. Schools that treat retention as a system to be deliberately designed and continuously improved—rather than a crisis to react to after the resignation letter arrives—spend less time rebuilding rosters and more time delivering on the promise that brought families to their doors in the first place.
