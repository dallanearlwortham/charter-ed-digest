How Charter Boards Drive—or Derail—School Quality

# How Charter School Boards Drive—or Derail—School Quality

The most visible faces of a charter school are usually the principal, the teachers, and the students. But behind every thriving charter—or every one that quietly unravels—is a governing board making decisions that ripple into every classroom. Research and real-world closures alike tell the same story: board governance is one of the most consequential and least-discussed variables in charter school success.

The Board's Job Is Not to Run the School

One of the most common governance errors charter boards make is conflating oversight with management. The distinction matters enormously. A charter school board sets policy, approves the budget, and—most critically—hires and evaluates the school leader. It does not make program-level decisions, direct staff, or insert itself into daily operations.

When boards cross that line, problems tend to follow. Well-documented cases of charter school instability—high principal turnover, staff exodus, plummeting morale—frequently trace back to a board that substituted its own judgment for the school leader's, or failed to define clear performance expectations in the first place. Research on principal burnout in charter settings consistently finds that the demands of managing upward to a board and downward to teachers and families simultaneously is a significant driver of early departure.

The core governance principle: the board holds the school leader accountable for results, not for the methods used to achieve them.

What Strong Charter Boards Actually Do Differently

A Fordham Institute study examining charter board governance in Washington, D.C., found meaningful differences between the boards of higher- and lower-performing schools. The findings are instructive for charter leaders and families anywhere:

  • They know their school's data. Board members at higher-performing schools were significantly more knowledgeable about their school's academic performance ratings, demographic profile, and financial outlook than their counterparts at lower-performing schools.
  • They use staff satisfaction when evaluating leaders. Boards at stronger schools factored in teacher and staff satisfaction as part of their school leader evaluations—recognizing that a leader who can't retain talent is a compounding risk to the school's future.
  • They participate in targeted training. There was a measurable relationship between board quality and participation in specific training—particularly around budget development and legal/policy compliance. Not generic nonprofit governance seminars, but charter-specific preparation.
  • They keep the workload manageable. High-functioning boards in the D.C. study met roughly every six to eight weeks, with members averaging about six hours per month on board service. Sustainability matters; a board that burns out its volunteers governs poorly.

The Stakes: When Boards Fail, Schools Close

This isn't an abstract concern. A comprehensive analysis of more than 2 million U.S. Department of Education records found that by year 20, the average charter school failure rate reaches 55%. Research from the National Center for Charter School Accountability and the Network for Public Education identified low enrollment and fraud or mismanagement as the most common reasons for charter closures in recent school years.

Both of those failure modes are governance problems at their root. A board that neglects enrollment trends until they become a crisis, or fails to maintain adequate financial oversight, is failing in its most basic fiduciary responsibilities.

In Minnesota, 2024 was a record year for charter school failures, with nine schools closing—and another ten threatened with the loss of their contracts by state-approved authorizers in the year that followed. In case after case, the precipitating factors included the same cluster of governance red flags: financial irregularities, leadership instability, and boards that were either disengaged or, conversely, too deep in the operational weeds to see the strategic picture.

Warning Signs Families and Leaders Should Watch For

What does a governance problem look like from the outside?

  • Frequent turnover of the school leader or senior staff within a short window
  • Board meeting agendas dominated by operational minutiae rather than strategic direction
  • Audits that are late, qualified, or reveal material weaknesses year over year
  • A board that cannot clearly articulate the school's mission or current academic standing
  • Visible conflict between the board chair and the school leader that spills over to staff or families

None of these signals in isolation is a death knell. But patterns matter—and families researching a charter school are well within their rights to ask about board composition, training practices, and how the board measures school leader performance.

Building a Board That Lasts

Public Charter School Alliance of South Carolina operates as the statewide advocacy and support organization for the charter school sector in South Carolina—dedicated, as its mission states, to ensuring every student in the state has access to a high-quality public school. The Alliance's model reflects what the research recommends: sustained, statewide infrastructure that supports charter school boards through training, resources, and a professional community, rather than leaving each of the state's 111 charter schools to navigate governance challenges in isolation.

That kind of ecosystem-level support matters because most charter board members arrive as volunteers with deep civic commitment but little sector-specific knowledge. The gap between good intentions and effective governance is real—and it can be closed with the right investment in board development.

The Right Question to Ask About Any Charter School

What makes a charter school board effective? The short answer, backed by research: effective boards are mission-focused, data-literate, financially disciplined, and clear on the line between setting direction and running operations. They hire strong leaders—and then trust those leaders enough to lead. They monitor outcomes without dictating methods. And they invest in their own training with the same seriousness they bring to academic accountability.

Charter school boards are, in a structural sense, where accountability lives. Authorizers hold schools accountable; boards hold school leaders accountable; school leaders hold teachers accountable. When that chain works, schools work. When it breaks anywhere along the line, the students in those seats pay the price.

For families choosing a charter school and for leaders building or joining one, the governance question deserves the same scrutiny as curriculum, test scores, or school culture. The board you never see in the hallway may be the single most important factor in whether the school your child attends is still thriving five years from now.
