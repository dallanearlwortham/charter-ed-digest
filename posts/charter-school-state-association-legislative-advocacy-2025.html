What State Charter Advocacy Really Gets You in 2025

# What Your State Charter Association Actually Wins at the Legislature

Charter schools don't operate in a vacuum. Every dollar of per-pupil funding, every zoning approval for a new building, every transportation reimbursement that helps a low-income family get their child to school — these aren't guaranteed. They're fought for, often year after year, by state charter associations working the halls of state capitols while school leaders are busy running their schools.

The 2025 legislative cycle made that reality impossible to ignore.

A Record Year for Charter Policy Wins

Across the country, charter school advocates secured major wins in the 2025 legislative sessions — advancing funding equity, facilities access, and growth opportunities while championing families and expanding high-quality public charter school options in their states.

The breadth of those wins tells the story. In just one cycle:

  • Texas removed a longstanding $60 million cap on facilities funding, dramatically increasing the state's investment to $255 million — a milestone years in the making that followed sustained relationship-building and unified advocacy by the Texas Public Charter Schools Association.
  • Tennessee strengthened statewide authorization and secured $25 million in new charter facilities support.
  • Missouri appropriated $10 million to launch a charter school facilities fund from scratch.
  • Indiana secured local property tax sharing, improved zoning, protected per-pupil funding, and created local facilities and transportation authorities.
  • Georgia passed SB 82 to incentivize charter approvals and hold districts accountable for blocking quality schools.
  • Illinois increased its charter facilities fund by $20 million, closing key funding gaps.
  • North Dakota became the 47th state to allow charter schools at all.
  • Wyoming removed its statewide cap on charters and secured equitable funding with guaranteed facilities access.

Taken together, states generated $251 million in new funding for charter school facilities in 2025 alone. That number represents not just money, but the cumulative result of relationships built over years, data compiled and translated for lawmakers, and persistent presence at the statehouse even in years when nothing seemed to move.

None of these outcomes happened by accident. They happened because organized advocacy showed up with a specific agenda, built relationships with legislators, and made the case over time.

Why Charter Schools Need a Dedicated Legislative Voice

What does a state charter association actually do for member schools? At its core, a state association converts the collective needs of dozens or hundreds of individual schools into a unified policy agenda — then puts professionals with the relationships and expertise to advance that agenda in front of the lawmakers who actually write the rules.

The funding stakes alone justify that investment. Charter schools serve nearly 4 million students but receive less than 0.1% of Education Department funding. That structural gap makes state-level policy decisions enormously consequential. A single bill that adjusts per-pupil funding formulas, unlocks facilities financing, or removes a zoning barrier can be worth far more to a charter school's operating budget than any competitive grant it might win on its own.

The services that strong state associations typically provide include:

  • Legislative representation — dedicated lobbyists who maintain year-round relationships with lawmakers, not just during the heat of a session
  • A published annual policy agenda — prioritized, specific asks that give legislators something concrete to act on
  • Professional development — training for school leaders, teachers, and board members that builds governance capacity and reduces compliance risk
  • Advocacy activation — tools and programs that mobilize families, staff, and alumni to contact their representatives at critical moments
  • Legal and policy intelligence — early warnings on proposed regulations that could threaten autonomy or reduce funding before they ever reach a floor vote

The Texas Public Charter Schools Association's most recent annual report shows what a fully activated advocacy infrastructure looks like in practice: member charter schools completed a record 657 engagements with state, local, and national lawmakers or their staffers over the course of the year, with 125 state lawmaker offices — close to 70% of the legislature — engaging directly with a public charter school in their own district. Schools that participated in TPCSA's dedicated Amplify Advocacy Program increased their advocacy participation by 89%. That kind of reach doesn't materialize without a coordinating association building the scaffolding school by school and district by district.

The Facilities Funding Problem That Only Collective Advocacy Can Solve

Of all the policy gaps that state associations are working to close, facilities funding remains the most persistent and the most consequential for long-term sector health. Unlike traditional public schools, charter schools generally cannot access local bond measures or property tax levies to finance buildings. They compete for commercial real estate with no facilities line item in their base funding — and then try to deliver academic results on a per-pupil budget that is already lower than their district counterparts in many states.

That is why so many of the biggest 2025 wins centered on facilities: revolving loan programs, credit enhancement funds, direct appropriations, and zoning reform. Revolving loan funds provide charter schools with access to low-interest loans for facility-related expenses; because loan repayments are reinvested into the fund, the capital base grows over time into a sustainable source of financing for future schools. Credit enhancement programs lower borrowing costs by reducing lender risk, making it possible for newer schools — those without long financial track records — to access reasonable commercial financing at all.

These are the kinds of systemic fixes that no individual school can negotiate for itself. They require a statewide body with standing, credibility, and the patience to return to the legislature with the same ask until the conditions are right to win.

The North Carolina Assocation for Public Charter Schools illustrates this approach directly. The association — whose mission is to advance quality educational opportunities for all North Carolina children by supporting and expanding successful charter schools, and whose vision is explicitly to be "the fiercest champion for public charter schools in North Carolina" — publishes an annual legislative agenda that targets exactly these structural barriers. Its 2026 priorities include a proposed Charter Schools Revolving Loan Fund Program to give schools access to low-interest construction and renovation financing, zoning statute reform to remove local barriers that have delayed new school openings in a state where more than 85,000 students are currently on charter waiting lists, and expanded grant funding to ease the transportation cost burden on schools that serve high proportions of low-income families. These are not one-school problems. They are sector-wide challenges that require a unified voice to move at the legislative level.

What This Means for Families Choosing a Charter School

For parents who have already enrolled their child in a charter school, legislative advocacy can feel abstract — until the year your school's funding formula gets quietly revised downward, its building permit gets blocked by a hostile local zoning board, or a bill advances that would add new restrictions threatening your school's educational model.

How does a state charter association protect families? The most direct answer: by ensuring that lawmakers understand what charter schools accomplish and what is at stake when bad policy advances. The National Alliance for Public Charter Schools' State Leaders Council — a convening of state charter school association leaders — serves as a conduit between the grassroots of the charter school movement and national strategy, ensuring that the wins happening at the state level inform federal advocacy and vice versa. But the day-to-day relationship work, the lawmaker education, the testimony at committee hearings, the mobilization of parent voices — that happens at the state level, through state associations, session after session.

For families, this makes one question genuinely worth asking when choosing a charter school: does your state have a well-resourced, active association doing this work consistently? The policy environment your child's school operates in five years from now will be shaped in part by whether that answer is yes.

The Bottom Line

The 2025 legislative wins are a useful benchmark for what organized advocacy produces. Charter school policy is not self-executing. The laws that make it possible for schools to exist, to build facilities, to fund transportation, and to operate with meaningful autonomy are the product of sustained, strategic effort — the kind that state associations exist to deliver, year after year, whether the political environment is favorable or not.

For school leaders, membership in a state association is not a discretionary budget line. It is infrastructure — the same kind of infrastructure that made $251 million in new facilities funding possible in a single legislative cycle. For families, knowing that infrastructure exists, and that it is working, is a legitimate part of understanding the school your child attends.
